JULY 10–13, 2026

Returning to the lake without giving Oliver back every freedom at once.

The lake was one of Oliver's favorite places, but it also included the exact kind of behavior I was trying to prevent: running, pivoting, slippery surfaces, and jumping from the dock. The goal was not to keep him from participating. It was to change how the weekend worked.

This page describes Oliver's personal experience. It does not establish when another dog is ready for travel, swimming, retrieving, sand, uneven ground, or dock access after TPLO.

THE DECISION

I wanted him included, but not unrestricted.

By the time we went to the lake, Oliver was active enough that completely excluding him from one of his favorite environments did not feel realistic. I still did not want him jumping from the dock or treating the trip like a normal pre-injury weekend.

The trip became a test of environmental management: could we change the people, the throwing location, and the routine enough that Oliver could participate without getting access to the highest-risk behavior?

WHAT WE ALLOWED

Participation with boundaries

Beach-based retrieving

Oliver was allowed to retrieve from the beach. Using the beach instead of the dock removed the high-impact takeoff he loved most.

Walking and visiting

He moved around the property, visited with family, and participated in normal lake activity in a more controlled way.

Frequent observation

I watched how he moved, how eager he was to continue, and whether he appeared tired or sore. The record does not contain precise distance, step counts, or session lengths.

Family consistency

Everyone followed the same rule: no one threw for Oliver from the dock. That consistency mattered more than expecting him to understand a temporary orthopedic restriction.

THE DOCK PROBLEM

Oliver kept showing me exactly what he wanted to do.

Each time it was time for another throw, Oliver went back to the dock. He loves jumping from it, and nothing about TPLO recovery changed that motivation.

I called him back to the beach every time. More importantly, the people around him changed their behavior too. No one rewarded the dock approach with a throw.

That experience taught me that management can be more reliable than self-control. Oliver did not need to understand why dock jumping was temporarily off-limits. We needed to make the safer option the only one available.

WHAT THE WEEKEND CHANGED

It rebuilt both of our confidence.

Oliver looked capable

He handled more movement, activity, and stimulation than I had been allowing at home.

I realized I had become very cautious

The trip showed me that my fear of overdoing it had started to shape the recovery as much as Oliver's actual condition.

The weekend was not medical clearance

Oliver's ability to participate did not prove bone healing or unrestricted readiness. Capacity and formal clearance remained separate.

The next step became rebuilding

After the trip, I was more willing to increase controlled walking and focus on restoring muscle and confidence.

WHAT REMAINS UNCERTAIN

I am not filling in what the record does not prove.

  • Whether Oliver had formal veterinary approval for the trip
  • How much actual swimming occurred
  • Whether swimming had been medically cleared
  • Exact retrieving-session length and frequency
  • Exact distance walked over the weekend
  • Whether he ran, pivoted, or used a long line
  • How much soreness or stiffness he had afterward
  • Which crate or recovery setup was used at the lake

WHAT I WOULD REPEAT

I would manage the environment instead of relying on willpower.

I would again move all throws to the beach, ask everyone to follow the same rule, and keep the highest-risk trigger from being rewarded.

I would also want clearer written guidance before the trip about swimming, sand, uneven ground, vehicle loading, total activity, and what signs should end the outing.

THE BIGGEST LESSON

Letting Oliver participate did not mean pretending he was fully recovered.

The lake trip worked because the boundaries stayed visible. He could be part of the weekend, but the dock remained off-limits. That balance helped both of us move forward.
