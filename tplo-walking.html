OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

How did I begin trusting Oliver to move again?

Progress came from controlled participation, next-day observation, and separating ability from veterinary clearance.

What this page is: Kristin's documented experience with Oliver, separated from veterinary information and remaining uncertainty.

The short answer

Walking progression was not a straight line. The stroller preserved Oliver's normal walk routine, short controlled walking was added as allowed, and I judged increases by how he looked afterward and the next morning—not only by what he could accomplish in the moment.

Why the stroller mattered

Oliver expected to join walks with Baxter. Leaving him behind created frustration and removed a major routine anchor. The stroller allowed him to come along, observe the neighborhood, and walk only the small portion that fit the current plan. It supported participation without turning the walk into uncontrolled exercise.

How I decided whether to increase activity

I separated three questions: Could Oliver physically do more? Had the veterinary team cleared more? Did he recover well afterward? A dog can demonstrate capacity before the repair is ready for unrestricted movement. I watched for increased limping later in the day, stiffness the next morning, unloading the leg, or a return of earlier compensation patterns.

The setback that changed my thinking

Late in June, Oliver repeatedly lifted the operated leg and I worried that recovery was not progressing. The available record points to veterinary reassurance that the repair looked stable and overuse or muscular discomfort was considered more likely, but this should remain tied to his actual evaluation rather than generalized to another dog.

What the lake trip showed

From July 10–13, Oliver tolerated substantially more walking, varied terrain, swimming, and normal exploration than I expected. That experience increased both his confidence and mine. It did not become formal clearance. It showed current capacity and helped me shift from protecting him from nearly everything toward rebuilding function more realistically.

The rule I would keep

Do not ask only, “How much can my dog do today?” Ask, “How much can my dog do today and still look good tomorrow morning?”
