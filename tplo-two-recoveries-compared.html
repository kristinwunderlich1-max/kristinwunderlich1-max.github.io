What did I do differently the second time Oliver needed TPLO surgery?

Experience improved preparation, but the second recovery still created different problems.

What this page is: Kristin's documented experience with Oliver, separated from veterinary information and remaining uncertainty.

The second recovery was easier to plan, but it was not a repeat.

I had just learned what Oliver needed during the first recovery. That gave me better equipment, faster decisions, and more confidence. It did not prevent new mistakes or guarantee that Oliver would behave the same way.

QuestionFirst TPLOSecond TPLO
Recovery spaceThe original pen felt tight once the bed, bowls, cone, and dog were inside. It changed repeatedly.I began with a larger pen, better airflow, and camera coverage, then quickly converted it into a flexible nook as Oliver chose nearby resting areas.
ComfortI learned that firm support, cool surfaces, airflow, and closeness could matter more than adding blankets.I had multiple beds and cooling options ready, but still followed Oliver's changing choices instead of insisting on one surface.
InclusionThe stroller and supervised freedom showed that Oliver tolerated participation better than long isolation.I reintroduced the stroller and household inclusion sooner because I already knew how strongly routine affected him.
Incision protectionCone-free periods expanded successfully as healing progressed.I forgot to put the cone back on after a cone-free period, and Oliver removed a couple of stitches. The process needed a visible cone station, not memory alone.
AnxietyStorms revealed that the basement safe space could not simply be removed from the plan.I planned earlier for sound, cameras, medication timing, and the conflict between stairs and emotional safety.
Owner confidenceEvery stage was unfamiliar and required more reassurance.I understood the mechanics of recovery better, but gait concerns and the unusually fast second injury created a different kind of fear.

What I bought or prepared differently

I started with the larger recovery space, a quieter fan, camera coverage, a FurHaven bed in the pen, the Big Barker upstairs, and a clearer sense that the pen would probably evolve. I also knew the stroller was not an indulgence; it preserved routine and inclusion while walking remained limited.

What I still got wrong

Experience did not make me immune to a simple process failure. Oliver had been cone-free while supervised, and I forgot to replace it before stepping away. He reached the incision and removed stitches. The mistake changed my recommendation: put the cone in a location that physically interrupts you before you leave the recovery area.

What surprised me most

The same dog could recover quickly in some ways and create a completely different problem in another. The second surgery confirmed that previous experience should improve preparation, not create false certainty.
