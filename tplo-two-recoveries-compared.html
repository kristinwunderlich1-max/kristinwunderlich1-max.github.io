THE SAME DOG. TWO RECOVERIES.

What I did differently the second time Oliver needed TPLO surgery

Having been through TPLO once did not make the second recovery automatic. It gave me better questions, more useful equipment, and a clearer understanding of Oliver—but the second surgery still created its own problems.

This is a comparison of my experience with one dog. It is not a recovery protocol or veterinary advice.

THE TWO SURGERIES

Experience changed the starting point, not the need to pay attention.

Oliver's first documented TPLO was on his right rear leg on April 16, 2026. His second was on the left rear leg on June 5, 2026. The recoveries happened close together, so many first-surgery lessons were still fresh when I prepared the house again.

The first recovery taught me how Oliver responded to confinement, heat, cone use, storms, stairs, stroller walks, and gradual freedom. The second recovery tested whether those lessons still applied—and showed me where relying on memory was not enough.

WHAT CHANGED

The second recovery began with fewer unknowns.

I planned a larger, more flexible recovery space.

The first pen changed repeatedly as I learned what Oliver actually needed. Before the second surgery, I started with more room, better airflow, a larger bed, and a camera angle that let me check him without disturbing him.

I included normal life earlier.

During the first recovery, Oliver showed me that supervised freedom and joining walks in the stroller helped him settle. The second time, I was less focused on keeping him inside a perfect pen and more focused on creating safe ways for him to remain part of the household.

I treated storms and fireworks as part of the recovery plan.

The first surgery taught me that his basement bunker was not optional in his mind. During the second recovery, noise anxiety, stairs, medication, cameras, and emotional safety were considered together rather than as separate problems.

I watched his choices instead of insisting on one bed.

Oliver used orthopedic beds, rugs, tile, the floor near a fan, and familiar sleeping spots at different times. The second time, I expected preferences to change and adjusted the setup around what helped him rest.

WHAT STILL SURPRISED ME

The cone mistake was a memory-system problem.

Oliver had already been cone-free for a while during the second recovery. On June 12, I forgot to put it back on before I stepped away, and he removed a couple of stitches.

The lesson was not that supervised cone-free time had been wrong. The problem was that putting the cone back on depended on memory alone. I needed the cone in a prominent, dedicated place so it was difficult to go upstairs, outside, or leave the house without seeing it.

What I would do now: create a visible cone station beside the recovery area or the path I use when leaving the room. Make “cone back on” part of the transition, not an afterthought.

WHAT REPEATED

Oliver was still Oliver.

Prior experience did not remove the parts of recovery tied to his personality and routines.

He wanted to be included.

Missing normal walks and household activity remained emotionally difficult. The stroller and modified activities helped him participate without pretending he was fully recovered.

He cared about temperature and airflow.

A quiet fan and access to cooler surfaces mattered in both recoveries, sometimes more than the bed I expected him to choose.

He challenged furniture and stair restrictions.

Recovery plans had to account for the couch, bedroom, basement, and favorite stair landings—not just the surgical leg.

He looked better before recovery was finished.

In both recoveries, the difficult phase shifted from helping an uncomfortable dog to protecting a dog who felt capable of doing more than the healing process allowed.

THE BIGGEST DIFFERENCE

I trusted observation more, but I did not stop checking.

The first recovery gave me a reference point. I knew what normal healing had looked like for Oliver. I knew confinement could become counterproductive for him, that the stroller preserved routine, and that storms could change the entire plan.

What I did not know was whether the second recovery would follow the same path. It did not always. Having experience helped me adapt faster, but it also reminded me not to assume the same dog would make the same choices after a different surgery.
