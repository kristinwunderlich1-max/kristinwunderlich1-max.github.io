WHAT WE ACTUALLY USED

The products that supported Oliver's TPLO recovery—and the ones he did not use as expected.

I did not need every recovery product marketed to dog owners. Some of the most useful things were items I already owned, and some of the nicest beds were ignored when Oliver preferred cool tile or the floor.

This is a record of what I used for Oliver. It is not a product ranking, medical recommendation, or substitute for your veterinarian's instructions.

THE MOST IMPORTANT LESSON

Start with the problem, not the product.

Each item made sense only in the context of a specific problem: limiting movement, creating a comfortable place to rest, keeping Oliver cool, reducing how much he had to walk, or helping me observe him without disturbing him.

I would not tell another owner to buy the same list. I would tell them to think through their dog's size, habits, temperature preferences, anxiety, home layout, and the instructions from their veterinary team.

CONFINEMENT

Exercise pen and crate decisions

Exercise pen

Problem I was solving: Oliver needed a confined recovery area without being limited to a crate all day.

What happened: The pen became his main recovery space. I later expanded it because he could safely use more room while still remaining restricted.

What worked: It was adaptable and gave him space for a large bed, water, and different resting positions.

Would I use it again? Yes.

36-inch and 42-inch crate planning

Problem I was solving: I needed a secure option for travel and the lake, especially with Oliver's fireworks anxiety.

What happened: A larger crate was considered for the lake context. The current records do not fully confirm which crate was ultimately used during the July trip.

What remains uncertain: Exact model, fit, delivery date, and Oliver's actual experience using it.

BEDS AND COOLING

The bed he chose was not always the one I expected.

FurHaven orthopedic bed

Problem I was solving: Oliver needed a large, supportive bed inside the recovery pen.

What happened: It fit the space and gave him a comfortable option, but he did not always choose it.

What worked: It was large enough for him to reposition comfortably.

What did not work as expected: A thick orthopedic bed was not automatically his preferred surface.

Big Barker bed

Problem I was solving: Oliver already had a familiar upstairs sleeping space.

What happened: He later chose this bed when he put himself to sleep upstairs after a long fireworks night.

Would I use it again? Yes, especially because it was already part of his normal routine.

Kuranda elevated bed

Problem I was solving: I wanted to give Oliver a cooler, elevated resting option.

What happened: When I placed it where he had been lying, he sometimes chose the floor beside it instead.

What I learned: A product can make sense to me and still not match what the dog wants in that moment.

Tile and hard floor

Problem it solved: Oliver often wanted a cooler surface.

What happened: He repeatedly chose tile or the floor despite having many beds available.

Would I provide it again? Yes, as long as the path to and from it was safe and traction was considered.

MOBILITY AND MONITORING

Products that helped me manage daily life

Dog stroller

Problem I was solving: I wanted Oliver to join outings without requiring him to walk the full distance.

What happened: The stroller acted as a temporary bridge during restricted activity.

What changed: Around the six-week point, I began allowing more walking after seeing how much controlled activity he could handle.

Would I use it again? Yes, for the same purpose.

Home cameras

Problem I was solving: I wanted to monitor Oliver without repeatedly disturbing him.

What happened: I added coverage for the basement bunker, the rest of the basement, and the bedroom.

What worked: Cameras helped me see where he was resting and notice behavior over time.

Important limit: A camera helped me observe; it did not tell me what a behavior meant medically.

Back-seat hammock

Problem I was solving: I needed a familiar, contained travel setup for both dogs in my Lexus RX350.

What happened: The hammock remained part of the rear-seat setup.

What still needed a better plan: Safe loading and unloading became much more important because the injury began during an attempted jump into the car.

Cooling orthopedic crate mats

Problem I was solving: I wanted cooler crate surfaces for the lake.

What happened: Two mats were ordered, but the current records do not fully confirm their long-term durability or exact use during the trip.

Would I recommend them? Not yet. There is not enough verified experience to make that claim.

SMALL THINGS THAT HELPED

Not every useful item was a special recovery product.

Fan outside the pen

The fan supported airflow while staying out of Oliver's reach. It was a simple adjustment that matched his preference for cooler resting conditions.

Partial blanket cover

A blanket over part of the pen created a more enclosed feel without closing off the space completely.

Raised water bowl

I considered a raised water setup to make drinking easier and reduce awkward movement. The current records do not preserve enough detail to call it essential.

Cheese for medication

During fireworks, Oliver would not always eat normally but would take pills wrapped in cheese. That was useful in our home, but it is not presented here as medication advice for another dog.

WHAT I WOULD BUY FIRST

The essentials were the things that solved the biggest practical problems.

For Oliver, that meant a safe confinement area, a comfortable place to rest, water within easy reach, a way to limit walking while still including him, and a monitoring setup that reduced unnecessary disturbance.

I would not begin with a long shopping list. I would begin by identifying the dog's actual needs and the limitations of the home.
