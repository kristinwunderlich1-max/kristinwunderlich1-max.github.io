OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

Which TPLO products and equipment were actually useful?

The useful items solved specific problems. None of them replaced observation or a workable household system.

What this page is: Kristin's documented experience with Oliver, separated from veterinary information and remaining uncertainty.

Everything is organized by the problem it was meant to solve.

This is one page so a stressed owner does not have to navigate multiple product reviews. Each item is explained in context: why I used it, what happened, and whether I would choose it again.

Building the recovery space

Exercise pen

Problem: Limit uncontrolled movement without isolating Oliver in a small crate.

Experience: The first configuration was too tight. The larger second setup worked better and could be opened into a nook.

Choose again? Yes, but larger from the start.

Camera

Problem: Monitor rest, licking, pacing, and basement behavior without repeatedly disturbing him.

Experience: Camera coverage reduced unnecessary checking and revealed behavior I might otherwise miss.

Choose again? Yes.

Partial cover and brown noise

Problem: Create security during storms and fireworks.

Experience: A partially covered area helped without blocking airflow; brown noise reduced environmental sound but did not replace the basement bunker.

Choose again? Yes, as part of a larger anxiety plan.

Keeping Oliver comfortable

Big Barker bed

Problem: Provide firm support upstairs and beside my bed.

Experience: Oliver used it at night and later chose it independently. It was supportive but not always the coolest option.

Choose again? Yes.

FurHaven orthopedic bed

Problem: Provide a large recovery surface in the main living area.

Experience: The size gave Oliver room to stretch and helped him rest more deeply, though he still chose floors when hot.

Choose again? Yes.

Quiet oscillating fan

Problem: Panting and heat made settling difficult.

Experience: Airflow became one of the most consistently useful parts of the setup.

Choose again? Absolutely.

Kuranda and cool floors

Problem: Give Oliver a cooler alternative.

Experience: He sometimes lay beside the elevated bed instead of on it. The lesson was to provide safe options and watch what the dog actually chooses.

Choose again? I would provide the option, not assume it will be preferred.

Helping Oliver move safely and stay included

Stroller

Problem: Preserve the shared walking routine without requiring the full walk.

Experience: It reduced frustration and allowed a controlled walking portion at the end.

Choose again? Yes. It was one of the most useful tools.

Lift harness or sling

Problem: Support transitions and stairs.

Experience: It was available as a safety aid, but handling a 55-pound dog still required planning and confidence.

Choose again? Yes, after confirming the proper use with the veterinary team.

Traction and couch steps

Problem: Reduce slipping and manage furniture access.

Experience: Blocking furniture did not always prevent Oliver from trying. A safer access strategy sometimes worked better than a barrier he would defeat.

Choose again? Traction, yes. Furniture access should follow the medical plan.

What did not work as expected

The most expensive or orthopedic option was not automatically the surface Oliver preferred. The smallest pen that looked adequate before surgery became crowded in real use. Blankets could trap heat or be pushed away. The product lesson was to solve the observed problem, not keep adding items because the setup looked incomplete.
