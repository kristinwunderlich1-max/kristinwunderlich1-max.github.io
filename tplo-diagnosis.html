OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

How did Oliver's two CCL diagnoses come together?

The first diagnosis required a pattern. The second followed a clear incident and moved quickly.

What this page is: Kristin's documented experience with Oliver, separated from veterinary information and remaining uncertainty.

The short answer

Oliver's two diagnoses followed different paths. The first required repeated observation, videos, examination, and imaging before the right partial tear became clear. The second moved quickly from a car-jump injury to a diagnosis of a complete left tear one week later.

The first diagnosis: a pattern had to be assembled

The right-leg problem did not begin with one obvious rupture. I first thought the changes might be related to hip arthritis. Oliver was removed from agility, his activity was adjusted, and I kept documenting how he moved at home. Because he did not always show the same discomfort in the clinic, the home videos helped demonstrate that the issue was functional and persistent.

The veterinary evaluation and knee imaging identified the right CCL problem. TPLO was chosen for an active dog of Oliver's size with a partial tear that was affecting normal life. The exact reasoning belongs to his veterinary records; the owner-level lesson is that a changing explanation is not a failure when new evidence becomes available.

The second diagnosis: the decision moved faster

On May 21, Oliver attempted to jump into the SUV and began limping. The limp did not resolve with rest. On May 28, the veterinarian diagnosed a complete left CCL tear. Having just completed the first surgery reduced uncertainty about the procedure, but it did not remove the practical burden of starting recovery again.

What to bring to the appointment

  • A timeline of when the problem started and how it changed
  • Videos from home, including turns and transitions after rest
  • A list of current medications and supplements
  • Questions about procedure choice, restrictions, stairs, car loading, follow-ups, and rehabilitation
  • Any concern that your dog masks pain or behaves differently in the clinic

What I wish someone had told me

A useful diagnosis gives you enough information to act. It does not answer every question about how the injury developed or exactly how recovery will unfold.
