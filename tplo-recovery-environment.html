RECOVERY ENVIRONMENT

How did I make our house work for TPLO recovery?

I did not build one perfect recovery room. I solved one practical problem at a time, then changed the setup when Oliver showed me that something else worked better.

This is not a universal shopping list. It is the equipment, existing furniture, household items, and adaptations that became part of Oliver's recovery. Some were extremely useful. Some had a limited role. Several still need more time before I would recommend them to another owner.

THE SHORT ANSWER

The environment worked because it could change.

The exercise pen gave us boundaries. The beds gave Oliver choices. The fan and cool flooring helped with temperature. Cameras helped me observe without repeatedly disturbing him. The stroller preserved participation when he could not walk normally.

No single product did everything. The most useful setup was a system that supported safety, comfort, routine, observation, and inclusion at the same time.

1. CREATING A SAFE RECOVERY SPACE

I chose an exercise pen instead of relying only on a crate.

I wanted Oliver to have enough room to lie down, reposition, drink, and rest without being able to wander through the house or make an impulsive jump.

The first configuration became too tight once I accounted for the large bed, water, cone, and turning space. I connected two exercise pens and expanded the area. They fit together without separate expansion hardware.

That was one of the first times recovery reminded me that preparation and reality are not the same thing. Preparing the pen before surgery still helped. Being willing to change it afterward helped more.

What we used

KennelMaster exercise pens

Two 36-inch, eight-panel wire pens connected to create a larger recovery area.

Purchased for recovery: Yes

What worked: More flexible than a crate while still limiting roaming.

Limitation: The original configuration did not leave enough usable space.

Would I use it again? Yes, but I would begin with the larger layout.

2. GIVING HIM MORE THAN ONE PLACE TO REST

The expensive orthopedic bed was useful. It was not always what Oliver wanted.

Oliver used orthopedic beds, but he also chose cool tile, hard flooring, a folded rug, and the space beside an elevated bed. His preference changed with temperature, location, security, and habit.

Purchased for recovery

FurHaven orthopedic bed

This was the main bed inside the recovery pen. It was large enough for Oliver and gave him a supportive place to stretch out.

What did not work as expected: A thick orthopedic bed did not automatically become his preferred surface, especially when he wanted to be cooler.

3. KEEPING OLIVER COOL

Temperature sometimes mattered more than softness.

Oliver repeatedly chose the area near the fan or a cooler floor instead of one of the many beds available to him.

I replaced a louder box fan with a quieter LEVOIT tower fan and positioned it outside the pen, where it could provide airflow without being within Oliver's reach.

The larger lesson was not “buy this fan.” It was to watch for temperature preference before interpreting floor-sleeping as rejection of a bed or lack of comfort.

Purchased for recovery

LEVOIT tower fan

Problem: Provide quieter airflow near the recovery pen.

What worked: Oliver repeatedly chose the area where the airflow reached him.

Limitation: It did not replace the need for multiple safe resting surfaces.

Would I use it again? Yes.

4. WATCHING WITHOUT CONSTANTLY INTERRUPTING HIM

The cameras helped me observe more and disturb him less.

I already had cameras in several parts of the house. During recovery, I added coverage for Oliver's basement bunker, the larger basement area where he sometimes rested, and the bedroom.

The cameras sat on shelves and counters. They were not mounted. A longer power cable made it possible to place one where the view was actually useful.

Cameras did not tell me whether something was medically wrong. They helped me notice where he settled, how long he rested, and whether a behavior was repeated or only happened once.

Purchased or expanded for recovery

Ring Indoor Cam Plus and 10-foot cable

Problem: Check on Oliver without repeatedly entering the room.

What worked: Better observation of long rest periods, nighttime behavior, and basement use.

Limitation: A camera is an observation tool, not a medical assessment.

5. PRESERVING PARTICIPATION

The stroller helped Oliver remain part of the walk.

I bought the stroller the year before TPLO, so it was not a recovery purchase. During recovery, it became a bridge between complete restriction and normal walking.

Oliver could come with us without having to walk the full route. That protected his activity while preserving something just as important to him: participation in the routine.

By July 16, after the lake trip increased both his confidence and mine, I let him walk instead of riding in the stroller. The stroller was useful because it was temporary—not because I wanted it to replace walking indefinitely.

Previously owned and repurposed

EchoSmile XL stroller

Problem: Keep Oliver included while limiting how much he had to walk.

What worked: Preserved his walking routine and family participation.

Limitation: It could not answer when he was medically ready for more walking.

Would I use it again? Yes, as a temporary bridge.

6. MAKING DAILY CARE EASIER

Some of the most useful solutions were ordinary and improvised.

Recovery added medication timing, feeding around a cone, observation, cleaning, and transportation to an already complicated day. I looked for ways to reduce the number of things I had to remember or physically adjust each time.

Purchased for recovery

Pill organizer and pill cutter

I bought a weekly organizer with four compartments per day and a pill cutter because Oliver's medication plan involved multiple times and split tablets.

What is confirmed: They were intended to reduce reliance on memory and simplify preparation.

What is still being evaluated: I have not yet finished documenting whether I would choose these exact models again.

Purchased plus household adaptation

Slanted bowl on Tupperware

I purchased a slanted stainless bowl and placed it on top of Tupperware to raise it. I did not buy a separate raised stand.

This is exactly the kind of recovery solution that would disappear from a polished product list: part purchased item, part ordinary household object.

Previously owned

4Knines backseat hammock

The hammock covered the back seat and the backs of the front seats in my SUV. It was already part of our travel setup and remained useful during recovery.

Limitation: It did not by itself solve the more important question of how to load Oliver safely after an injury that happened during an attempted jump into the vehicle.

Still being documented

Other purchased items

The sling, cone, gel packs, training cones, spring clamps, yard spray, larger crate, and several bedding items are part of the internal record. I am not presenting conclusions about them until their actual use and limitations are fully documented.

WHAT CHANGED MY THINKING

Observe before buying more.

Oliver had access to orthopedic beds and still chose tile. I placed a Kuranda where he liked to lie, and he sometimes chose the floor beside it. A stroller bought for another reason became one of the most useful recovery tools. Tupperware became a bowl riser. Cameras worked from shelves without mounts.

The products mattered, but their value depended on the actual problem in front of us. The most expensive or specialized option was not automatically the most useful one.

IF I WERE STARTING OVER

I would prepare a flexible system—not try to predict every purchase.

I would still prepare before surgery day. I would also leave room for the dog to show me which parts of the plan needed to change.

What I would make sure was ready

  • A safe, adjustable confinement area
  • At least one supportive bed and one safe cooler option
  • Water that can be reached while wearing the prescribed incision protection
  • A quiet airflow plan if the dog tends to run warm
  • A written medication system
  • A safe route for bathroom trips
  • A way to observe without constantly disturbing rest
  • A plan for preserving routine and inclusion
  • A transportation and vehicle-loading plan
  • Permission to change the setup when the dog shows what works

A NOTE ABOUT PRODUCT LINKS

The links identify what I used. They are not promises about what another dog needs.

These are not affiliate recommendations. Product listings, models, and availability can change. More importantly, another dog may need a completely different setup.

Before buying recovery equipment, consider your dog's size, behavior, temperature preference, mobility, anxiety, veterinary restrictions, and the layout of your own home.
