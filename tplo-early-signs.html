EARLY SIGNS

Could the small changes I am noticing actually mean something?

Sometimes they do. Sometimes they do not. The useful lesson from Oliver's experience is not to diagnose a CCL injury at home. It is to pay attention to repeated changes, preserve what you are seeing, and share the pattern with your veterinarian.

This page describes my observations and how understanding changed over time. It is not a symptom checklist, diagnosis guide, or substitute for a veterinary examination.

THE BIGGEST LESSON

The TPLO experience did not teach me to observe Oliver better.

I had always watched Oliver closely. I noticed how he walked, stood, sat, rested, climbed, jumped, and changed position.

What changed was not my ability to observe him. It was my understanding of what those observations might mean.

Small behaviors that had once seemed separate eventually became part of a larger picture. That did not make the answer obvious in the moment. It made the earlier observations more meaningful later.

The practical takeaway: do not dismiss repeated changes simply because each one seems small on its own.

AT FIRST, THERE WAS NO DRAMATIC INJURY

Something looked different, but the explanation was not clear.

Oliver's first CCL problem did not begin with one unmistakable event that told me exactly what was wrong. The changes were gradual and inconsistent.

I saw him protect one side when sitting. His weight-bearing was not always the same. Sometimes he was more hesitant when getting up. There were good days and worse days rather than one constant, severe limp.

None of those observations told me, by themselves, that Oliver had a partial CCL tear. They told me that something was not moving the way it normally did.

WHAT SEEMED MOST LIKELY AT THE TIME

Hip arthritis was a reasonable explanation.

At the time, hip arthritis seemed like the most likely explanation for what I was seeing. We did not know for certain, but it was the explanation that made the most sense.

Oliver was an active dog with years of agility, retrieving, swimming, and ordinary wear on his body. The changes did not initially look like a sudden complete knee injury.

I took him out of agility for several months to let him rest. I also switched him from Dasuquin to Rejensa, a prescription joint supplement, because arthritis seemed like the most likely problem.

Those decisions were based on the information available then. They were not proof that the knee had been overlooked, and they were not unreasonable in hindsight.

What I did while the cause remained uncertain

  • Stopped agility
  • Reduced the activity that seemed most demanding
  • Changed joint support under the working arthritis explanation
  • Continued watching how Oliver sat, stood, and moved
  • Took videos when the behavior appeared at home
  • Kept revisiting the explanation when the pattern continued

WHY THE VIDEOS MATTERED

Oliver did not always show the same behavior during appointments.

Oliver was exceptionally good at hiding discomfort, especially at the veterinary clinic.

I could describe what I saw at home, but descriptions are imperfect. A video preserved the actual gait, weight shift, sit, or transition instead of relying on my memory or wording.

The videos did not diagnose the injury. They gave the veterinarian a way to see what Oliver was doing outside the clinic when he was not displaying the same behavior during the examination.

They became one part of the overall picture, alongside the physical examination and diagnostic imaging.

What makes a home video more useful

  • Show the dog walking toward and away from the camera
  • Include a side view when possible
  • Record getting up after rest
  • Record sitting and standing transitions
  • Use the same surface and angle for later comparison
  • Note the date, recent activity, and whether medication was given
  • Do not manipulate the leg to create a response

THE PATTERN BECAME CLEARER

No single observation carried the whole story.

Protective sitting

Oliver sometimes sat in a way that appeared to protect one side instead of placing his weight evenly.

I could see the difference, but I could not determine the cause from posture alone.

Inconsistent weight-bearing

He used the leg, but not always in the same way. At times, the foot made only light contact.

The inconsistency made the problem harder to interpret than a constant non-weight-bearing limp.

Hesitation after rest

Getting up could look more careful or guarded than his movement after he had been moving for a while.

That was useful to preserve on video because it could disappear by the time of an appointment.

Good days did not erase the pattern

Oliver could still have days when he looked much closer to normal.

Improvement in one moment did not explain why the same protective behaviors kept returning.

Rest and supplements did not resolve it

Stopping agility and changing joint support did not fully explain or remove the repeated movement changes.

That was one reason the original working explanation had to be revisited.

One worse moment raised the concern

There was also a point when Oliver would not bear weight normally after getting up.

A more dramatic episode inside a gradual pattern made it harder to keep treating the issue as a simple fluctuation.

WHAT THE DIAGNOSTICS ADDED

More than one thing was happening.

The knee evaluation ultimately identified a partial right CCL tear.

The X-rays also showed arthritis beginning in Oliver's hips.

That mattered because the earlier arthritis explanation was not imaginary. Hip changes were present. They simply did not explain the entire pattern.

Looking back, it would be easy to rewrite the story as though everyone should have known the knee was the problem from the beginning. That would not be accurate.

The understanding developed over time because the observations continued, the videos preserved them, and the diagnostic picture eventually became clearer.

LOOKING BACK

The first explanation was incomplete, not foolish.

Hindsight puts all the pieces on the table at once. Real life does not.

At the time, I had subtle movement changes, an active older dog, no dramatic injury event, and a reasonable concern about arthritis.

Later, I had repeated videos, persistent protective movement, a partial CCL diagnosis, and imaging that also confirmed early hip arthritis.

Understanding changed because the evidence changed. That is different from saying someone missed an obvious answer.

WHAT I WOULD DO AGAIN

I would keep recording the small changes.

I would still take videos, especially when Oliver did something at home that he might not repeat at the clinic.

I would still reduce an activity that seemed to be making him uncomfortable while we worked toward a clearer answer.

I would still allow the explanation to change instead of forcing every new observation to fit the first theory.

WHAT I WOULD DOCUMENT MORE DELIBERATELY

I would make the pattern easier to review.

  • Date each video
  • Record whether the dog had just awakened, walked, played, or taken medication
  • Use comparable camera angles and surfaces
  • Note whether the behavior was occasional or repeated
  • Write down which activities had already been reduced
  • Track whether rest or joint support changed the pattern
  • Bring the videos and notes to the appointment
  • Ask what the current explanation does—and does not—account for

WHEN TO CALL THE VETERINARIAN

A pattern does not need to be dramatic before it is worth sharing.

Contact your veterinarian when a change persists, repeats, worsens, or affects how your dog bears weight, rises, sits, uses stairs, jumps, or participates in normal activities.

Seek more urgent guidance when your dog suddenly refuses to bear weight, experiences a major worsening, appears significantly painful, or has another concerning change.

The goal of observation is not to decide which ligament or joint is responsible. It is to give the veterinary team a clearer record of what changed and when.

WHAT THIS PAGE CANNOT ANSWER

The same behavior can have more than one cause.

Protective sitting, stiffness, an altered gait, hesitation, or intermittent limping can occur for different reasons. Oliver's later diagnosis does not turn those behaviors into a CCL test for another dog.

This page can help an owner notice, preserve, and communicate a pattern. It cannot identify the diagnosis.
