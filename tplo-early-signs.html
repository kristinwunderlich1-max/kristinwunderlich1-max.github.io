OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

Could the changes I am noticing mean a CCL injury?

Repeated changes in movement matter, but no home observation can confirm the diagnosis.

What this page is: Kristin's documented experience with Oliver, separated from veterinary information and remaining uncertainty.

The short answer

The most useful early signs were not one dramatic symptom. They were repeated changes in how Oliver sat, stood, rose, turned, and used a rear leg at home. Videos helped the veterinarian see behavior Oliver often hid in the clinic, but the videos did not tell us which structure was injured.

What I noticed during the first knee problem

The first right-knee problem developed gradually. Oliver sometimes protected the leg when sitting, shifted his weight, and moved differently after rest. Hip arthritis initially seemed like a reasonable explanation, so I stopped agility, adjusted activity, and kept watching. The pattern continued, which made a focused knee evaluation more important.

That experience taught me to record the movement that looked wrong instead of relying on memory. A short clip of rising, walking away from the camera, turning, and sitting gave the veterinary team a more realistic picture than a single office walk.

What was different the second time

The left-knee injury followed a clear event on May 21, 2026, when Oliver tried to jump into the SUV. The limp persisted. Because he had just been through a right TPLO, the possibility of another CCL injury was easier to recognize, but I still could not determine the severity at home. The complete tear was diagnosed by the veterinarian on May 28.

What I would do now

  • Restrict jumping and uncontrolled activity while waiting for the appointment.
  • Take short videos on familiar flooring, especially after rest and during turns.
  • Write down when the change began, whether it improves, and what makes it worse.
  • Ask the veterinarian to evaluate the knee specifically if the pattern persists.
  • Avoid manipulating the knee or trying to classify the tear yourself.

What surprised me most

The same dog had one knee problem that developed gradually and another that followed a clear incident. There was no single presentation I could treat as “what a CCL tear looks like.”
