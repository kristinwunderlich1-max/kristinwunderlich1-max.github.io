THE FIRST 72 HOURS

What is the first night after TPLO surgery really like?

For me, the first night was not one dramatic emergency. It was a long series of small decisions: watching, listening, giving medication, helping Oliver outside, and trying to understand what each sound or movement meant.

This page describes Oliver's experience. Follow your own veterinarian's discharge instructions and contact them if you are concerned about pain, medication effects, the incision, mobility, or any sudden change.

THE SHORT ANSWER

The first night felt less like “recovery” and more like learning a new language.

Oliver could not tell me whether he was uncomfortable, groggy, anxious, thirsty, or simply needed to go outside. I had to pay attention to the small signals and respond without assuming every unusual behavior meant the same thing.

I watched his breathing, his ability to settle, how he positioned himself, whether he drank, whether he needed help getting up, and whether he was able to urinate and defecate.

The first 72 hours were not about knowing exactly what would happen next. They were about staying close enough to notice when something changed.

SURGERY DAY

Oliver came home after his second TPLO on June 5, 2026.

His left rear knee had been repaired, no meniscus damage was found, and two shoulder masses were also removed during the procedure.

That meant I was watching more than one surgical area while also managing the effects of anesthesia, the beginning of the medication schedule, and the practical problem of getting a 56-pound dog settled safely at home.

That evening, I gave the medications listed in his discharge plan for that time, including gabapentin, acepromazine, and cephalexin.

I am deliberately not presenting Oliver's medication doses as a model for another dog. His schedule was prescribed for him, and even within his own records, several medication strengths and timing changes required later reconciliation.

What I was watching

  • Could he settle?
  • Was he able to stand with help?
  • Did he need to urinate?
  • Was he drinking?
  • Did his behavior change after medication?
  • Was he trying to reach the incision?
  • Was anything suddenly worse?

THE FIRST BIG RELIEF

He urinated—a lot.

One of the first concrete things I could confirm was that Oliver was able to urinate after coming home. It was a large amount.

That may sound like an unimportant detail until you are standing outside with a newly postoperative dog, supporting him, watching every step, and waiting to see whether his body is doing something ordinary.

In those first hours, ordinary things felt significant.

12:48 A.M.

Oliver whined, and I did not immediately know why.

A whine during the first night could have meant pain. It could also have meant fear, restlessness, confusion, medication effects, or a bathroom need.

I responded by taking him outside.

He urinated and defecated.

The immediate distress signal resolved after the bathroom trip. That did not prove he had no discomfort. It simply told me what that particular whine had meant.

This became one of the earliest examples of something I kept learning throughout recovery: do not decide what a behavior means before checking the simplest explanation.

WHAT MADE THE FIRST NIGHT HARD

Every ordinary need required a decision.

Going outside

A bathroom trip was no longer just opening the door. I had to help him rise, control the route, watch his footing, and keep the trip short.

Giving medication

Medication timing began immediately, and the schedule extended into the night. The plan had to be both veterinarian-approved and realistic enough for me to follow accurately.

Interpreting restlessness

I could not assume that movement or whining automatically meant pain. I had to look at the entire situation: time since the last bathroom trip, medication timing, body position, breathing, and whether he settled afterward.

Letting him rest

Close monitoring can become constant interference. I wanted to notice changes without repeatedly waking, moving, or touching him when he had finally found a comfortable position.

WHAT HELPED MOST

I wrote things down instead of trusting myself to remember them.

The first night included medication times, bathroom trips, behavior changes, and questions I might need to ask later. None of those details felt difficult to remember in the moment. Together, they became too much.

Recording what happened helped me separate what I actually observed from what I was afraid might happen.

It also made patterns easier to see. A sound that felt alarming in isolation became more understandable when I could connect it to the time of the last bathroom trip or a recent medication.

A PRACTICAL FIRST-NIGHT LOG

These are the details I would record again.

This is not a medical monitoring protocol. It is a practical record that can help an owner remember what happened and communicate more clearly with the veterinary team.

  • Time each prescribed medication was given
  • Food and water intake
  • Urination and bowel movements
  • Ability to stand and walk with the required support
  • Where and how the dog settled
  • Whining, panting, trembling, or unusual restlessness
  • Incision observations without repeatedly disturbing the dog
  • Questions to ask the veterinarian

WHAT CHANGED MY THINKING

Not every signal needed an answer immediately. Every signal did deserve attention.

Before the first night, I thought careful monitoring meant knowing what every behavior meant. It did not.

Sometimes careful monitoring meant noticing something, checking the obvious possibilities, watching what happened next, and writing down the question if I still did not understand it.

That distinction helped me avoid turning every uncertain moment into a crisis while still taking Oliver seriously.

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY

I would make the overnight plan more explicit before surgery.

  • Write the entire medication schedule before pickup
  • Confirm which medications begin that evening and which begin the next day
  • Decide who will handle each overnight bathroom trip
  • Keep a leash, support equipment, shoes, and a flashlight beside the recovery area
  • Prepare a simple log before the dog comes home
  • Write down the veterinarian's after-hours contact information
  • Ask which changes require an immediate call rather than waiting until morning

WHAT THIS PAGE CANNOT TELL YOU

Oliver's first night is not a checklist of what another dog should experience.

I cannot tell another owner how much panting, sleepiness, whining, swelling, appetite change, or difficulty walking is expected for their dog. Those questions belong with the veterinarian or surgeon who knows the procedure, medications, and individual dog.

I can only describe what I observed, what I did, and what I learned from paying attention to Oliver.
