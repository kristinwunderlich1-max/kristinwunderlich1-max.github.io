THE FIRST 72 HOURS

What should I expect when my dog first comes home after TPLO?

Expect to spend the first few days doing less than you imagined and watching more than you expected. The important work is simple: follow the discharge instructions, keep the medication plan organized, help with basic needs, and learn what your dog looks like after surgery.

This is Oliver's personal experience, not a recovery schedule or veterinary advice. Follow your own veterinarian's discharge instructions and call them when something feels wrong.

THE MOST USEFUL THING TO KNOW

You will not understand every sound or movement immediately.

During the first night, almost everything Oliver did felt important because I did not yet know what normal looked like for him after TPLO.

Was he uncomfortable? Did he need to go outside? Was he too warm? Was he reacting to medication? Was he simply trying to find a better position?

The answer was not always obvious. I learned to check the basic needs, look at the whole situation, and avoid deciding what one behavior meant before I had more information.

What I kept checking

  • Could he settle and rest?
  • Did he need to urinate or defecate?
  • Was he eating and drinking enough to take medications as directed?
  • Could he move safely with assistance?
  • Was the cone interfering with food, water, or rest?
  • Did the incision or surrounding area look meaningfully different?
  • Was he too warm, too cold, or unable to get comfortable?
  • Did the behavior improve after I addressed a basic need?

THE FIRST NIGHT

A whine at 12:48 a.m. taught me not to guess too quickly.

After Oliver's second TPLO, he had a large urination after coming home. Later that night, at approximately 12:48 a.m., he whined.

I did not know whether the sound meant pain, restlessness, confusion, or something else. I took him outside, and he urinated and defecated.

In that moment, the whine was useful communication about a bathroom need. The immediate distress resolved after the trip outside.

That does not mean whining after surgery should be dismissed or assumed to be harmless. It means one unfamiliar behavior does not always explain itself. I had to check what Oliver needed and keep the veterinary instructions close by.

When something seems wrong

  • Pause and look at the whole situation
  • Check bathroom needs
  • Check temperature and comfort
  • Review medication timing and discharge instructions
  • Look for a meaningful change in movement or the incision
  • Call the veterinary team when you are unsure

BEFORE THE DOG COMES THROUGH THE DOOR

Have the first few hours already planned.

I set up Oliver's recovery area while he was in surgery so I would not be assembling equipment after bringing home a dog who had just had major surgery.

The pen, bed, waterproof layer, water, fan, and clear path outside were ready before pickup. I also had the evening medication instructions where I could see them.

The setup did not have to remain exactly the same. It only had to make the first hours manageable while I learned what Oliver needed.

THE FIRST-DAY PRIORITIES

The basics mattered more than trying to predict every problem.

Medication

I followed the prescription labels and discharge instructions written specifically for Oliver. I did not rely on a generic TPLO schedule.

With several medications and changing intervals, one written chart is much safer than trying to remember what comes next.

Bathroom trips

Bathroom needs started immediately. I treated each trip as a slow, supervised task instead of waiting until Oliver was restless enough to make the need obvious.

A clear path and a plan for assistance mattered more than speed.

Food and water

Eating and drinking affected comfort and medication administration. The cone could make both awkward, so bowl placement and the amount of room around the bowls mattered.

I watched what Oliver actually managed instead of assuming the setup worked because it looked fine.

Comfort

Comfort was not only about having a good bed. Oliver also needed airflow, enough room to reposition, and the ability to settle without being repeatedly disturbed.

A dog can have several good beds and still choose a cooler or firmer surface later.

Incision awareness

I paid attention to the incision and the surrounding area, but I did not try to turn every visual difference into a diagnosis.

Photos helped me compare changes over time and gave me something concrete to share if I needed to call.

Rest

Rest did not always mean completely still. Oliver shifted positions, woke up, needed bathroom trips, and reacted to sounds.

The useful question was whether he could settle again after the need or interruption was addressed.

ONE SYSTEM I WOULD INSIST ON

Use one medication and observation record from the beginning.

TPLO recovery can involve several medications with different doses, intervals, start times, and stop dates. The plan can also change.

During Oliver's recoveries, medication details appeared across prescription labels, discharge papers, notes, and conversations. That made the record harder to reconstruct later than it should have been.

If I were starting again, I would create one sheet directly from the current labels and veterinary instructions. I would write down each dose as it was given and update the sheet whenever the plan changed.

I would keep observations on the same page: food, water, bathroom trips, sleep, incision photos, and anything that prompted a call.

What I would track

  • Medication name, strength, dose, and time given
  • Food and water intake
  • Urination and bowel movements
  • Ability to settle and sleep
  • Movement and weight-bearing observations
  • Incision photos and changes
  • Questions for the veterinary team
  • Any instructions that changed

WHAT THE FIRST 72 HOURS ARE REALLY FOR

You are establishing a baseline, not proving the recovery is perfect.

I was watching everything because I needed to understand what Oliver looked like after surgery. That baseline helped me notice when something improved, stayed the same, or changed enough to ask about.

The first days were not a test I could pass by keeping every detail under control. They were a period of learning what he needed while following the plan we had been given.

That is why documentation helped. A photo, a video, or a written time gave me something more reliable than trying to remember exactly what happened during a tiring night.

WHAT CHANGED AFTER THE FIRST NIGHT

The first-day setup did not have to last forever.

The waterproof blanket had done its job

I placed it over the bed because I was worried about urination while anesthesia was still affecting Oliver.

He did not pull it off until later, after the anesthesia had worn off and he no longer needed it.

The pen could be adjusted

Once the large bed, water, and cone were all part of the space, I could see whether Oliver had enough room to reposition comfortably.

The recovery area was a starting point, not a permanent design.

Supervised normal moments mattered

During the first recovery, Oliver had brief supervised time outside the pen, cone-free meals, outdoor time, and closeness with the family as the days progressed.

Those moments were still controlled, but they helped recovery feel less like complete removal from normal life.

The dog in front of me mattered more than the day number

Two recoveries in the same dog did not unfold in exactly the same way.

Dates helped me organize what happened, but they did not replace the veterinary plan or what Oliver was showing me.

WHAT I WOULD DO AGAIN

I would keep the first response simple.

I would have the environment ready, use one written medication record, take photos, keep the path outside clear, and stay close enough to respond without repeatedly disturbing him.

When Oliver did something unfamiliar, I would again check the basic needs before deciding what the behavior meant.

I would also call rather than guess when the situation did not improve, did not fit the discharge instructions, or simply felt wrong.

IF YOUR DOG IS COMING HOME TODAY

Focus on the next few hours, not the entire recovery.

  • Put the discharge instructions and medication chart where you can reach them
  • Make sure the resting area, water, and path outside are ready
  • Take a baseline photo of the incision if your veterinary team allows it
  • Write down medication times as you give them
  • Track food, water, urination, and bowel movements
  • Watch whether your dog can settle after a need is addressed
  • Keep the evening quiet and reduce predictable excitement
  • Call the veterinary team when you are unsure rather than trying to diagnose at home

WHAT THIS PAGE CANNOT TELL YOU

Only your veterinary team can tell you whether a change is medically concerning.

Oliver's first-night whine turned out to be connected to a bathroom need. Another dog's whine, restlessness, swelling, refusal to eat, or change in movement may mean something different.

This page can help you prepare, document, and ask better questions. It cannot determine whether your dog's recovery is progressing safely.
