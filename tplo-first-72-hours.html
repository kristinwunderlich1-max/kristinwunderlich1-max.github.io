OLIVER'S FIRST DAYS HOME

The first 72 hours after TPLO: what the first night was really like.

The first night was less dramatic than I feared, but it required constant attention. I was watching Oliver's comfort, medications, movement, bathroom needs, and ability to settle while trying not to interpret every unfamiliar behavior as a complication.

This is Oliver's personal experience, not a recovery schedule or veterinary advice. Follow your own veterinarian's discharge instructions and call them when something feels wrong.

THE IMMEDIATE ANSWER

What surprised me most

The most useful lesson from Oliver's first night was that a strange sound or restless moment did not automatically tell me what was wrong. At 12:48 a.m., he whined. I took him outside, and he urinated and defecated. In that moment, the whine was useful communication—not proof of a surgical problem.

That does not mean postoperative whining should be dismissed. It means I had to slow down, check the basics, observe the whole situation, and keep the veterinary discharge instructions close by.

JUNE 5, 2026

Bringing Oliver home

Oliver came home after his left TPLO surgery on June 5. The surgeon reported that there was no meniscus damage. Two shoulder lumps were also removed, which meant I was monitoring more than one surgical area.

The recovery space was already waiting

I had prepared an exercise-pen recovery area with a large orthopedic bed, nearby water, a fan positioned outside the pen, and a partial blanket cover. The setup gave Oliver a defined place to rest without relying only on a crate.

I was watching everything

I paid attention to whether he could settle, whether he needed to go outside, how he moved, and how he responded after coming home. The first night felt like a series of small observations rather than one clear moment when I knew everything was fine.

Medication details stayed specific to Oliver

I followed the prescriptions and instructions provided for Oliver. Because the internal record still contains conflicting strengths and incomplete timing details, this page does not reproduce a medication schedule.

Bathroom needs mattered immediately

Oliver produced a large urination after coming home. Later that night, his whine led to another bathroom trip. I learned quickly that bathroom needs had to remain part of every comfort check.

THE FIRST NIGHT

A simple sequence I still remember

  • Oliver came home and settled into the recovery area.
  • I gave the medications prescribed for that evening.
  • He had a large initial urination.
  • I continued watching his movement, comfort, and ability to rest.
  • At approximately 12:48 a.m., he whined.
  • I assisted him outside.
  • He urinated and defecated.
  • The immediate distress resolved after the bathroom trip.

I do not remember that night as calm simply because nothing catastrophic happened. I remember it as manageable because I had prepared the space, stayed close, and responded to what Oliver was actually showing me.

WHAT THE RECORD DOES AND DOES NOT SHOW

The first night is well documented. The full 72 hours are not.

The source record is strongest for surgery day and the first overnight period. It does not yet contain a complete hour-by-hour account of Oliver's food intake, water intake, incision appearance, sleep, bathroom trips, or every medication time across all three days.

Rather than fill those gaps with assumptions, this page stays with what is documented. Future updates can add more detail if dated notes, photos, discharge paperwork, or other records are verified.

WHAT HELPED ME MOST

Practical choices that made the first days easier

Preparing before pickup

The pen, bed, water, cooling, and walking path were ready before Oliver came home. I was not trying to assemble his main recovery space while also managing a sedated dog.

Keeping the response simple

When Oliver whined, I checked what he might need instead of immediately deciding what the sound meant. That helped me respond to the dog in front of me.

Allowing more than one comfortable surface

Oliver did not always prefer the thickest bed. As recovery continued, cool tile and harder surfaces sometimes appealed to him. His preferences helped shape the setup.

Accepting that close observation was work

Monitoring was not passive. It meant noticing changes, helping him outside, managing confinement, and deciding when a question belonged with the veterinarian.

WHAT I WOULD DO AGAIN

I would prepare the environment first and keep one clear record.

I would again have the recovery space ready before surgery, keep several safe resting options available, and respond to unusual behavior by checking basic needs first.

I would also create one reconciled medication and observation sheet directly from the veterinary labels and discharge instructions. The internal record became difficult to reconstruct later because different strengths, intervals, and notes appeared across several conversations.

THE NEXT USEFUL STEP

You do not have to understand every behavior immediately.

Prepare the space, follow the discharge plan, observe closely, and call your veterinary team when you are uncertain. The first days are a series of small decisions, and careful attention matters more than pretending to feel completely confident.
