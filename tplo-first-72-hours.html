OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

What are the first 72 hours after TPLO really like?

Expect a repetitive care cycle rather than a polished routine. Your job is safety, basic needs, observation, and calling when something falls outside the plan.

What this page is: Kristin's documented experience with Oliver, separated from veterinary information and remaining uncertainty.

The short answer

The first 72 hours were mostly a cycle of medication, sleep, bathroom trips, food, water, incision protection, and watching whether Oliver could settle. I did not need to create a perfect routine immediately. I needed a written schedule, a safe space, and a low threshold for contacting the veterinary team when something looked outside the instructions.

What mattered most

Comfort and rest: Oliver panted, changed positions, sought cooler surfaces, and sometimes needed closeness before he could settle. Airflow, bed choice, and quiet mattered. The first recovery also showed that he could want firm support rather than the softest available bed.

Food, water, and bathroom needs: The cone made bowls harder to use. Bathroom signals were not always obvious. During the second first night, a whine around midnight led to a successful urine and bowel movement rather than a surgical emergency.

Inclusion: Oliver was frustrated when Baxter left for a normal walk. The stroller later allowed him to join walks without completing the walking portion himself. Early inclusion helped preserve routine without pretending he was ready for normal activity.

Incision protection: The cone had to be treated as part of the environment. During the second recovery, Oliver later removed a couple of stitches because I forgot to put the cone back on before stepping away. The lesson was not that supervised cone-free time had been wrong; it was that the transition back to protection depended on memory alone.

The cone system I would use now

I would create a visible cone station beside the recovery area or the doorway I used when leaving. The cone would go there every time it came off. Before going upstairs, outside, or out of the house, the visible cone would act as the reminder to put it back on.

When I would call

I would call for any concern listed in the discharge instructions, including a change in the incision, uncontrolled pain, repeated vomiting, inability to urinate, severe instability, or behavior that did not fit the expected recovery plan.
