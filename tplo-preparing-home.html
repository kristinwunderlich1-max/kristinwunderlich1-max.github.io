BEFORE OLIVER CAME HOME

How I prepared the house for TPLO recovery—and why the setup kept changing.

I knew Oliver would need a controlled place to rest, safe bathroom trips, help with stairs, and a way to stay comfortable without roaming the house. I prepared as much as I could before surgery, but the most useful part of the setup was not any single product. It was being willing to adjust the plan after I saw what Oliver actually needed.

This page describes our home and Oliver's experience. Your veterinarian or surgeon should decide your dog's activity restrictions, confinement needs, stair use, and post-operative care.

THE QUESTION I WAS TRYING TO ANSWER

Where could a 55.8-pound dog safely recover without feeling completely shut away?

Oliver was already an active dog with strong preferences. He liked to move between rooms, sleep on different surfaces, follow what was happening in the house, and retreat to the basement when fireworks frightened him. After surgery, those normal habits became practical problems. I needed to limit uncontrolled movement while still giving him enough room to settle, change positions, drink, and feel connected to the household.

I also manage most of his care myself. That meant the plan had to work not only on paper, but during late-night bathroom trips, medication times, hot days, and moments when lifting or carrying a large dog was physically difficult.

THE RECOVERY SPACE

I chose an exercise pen as the main recovery area.

I wanted more flexibility than a crate alone would give us, while still preventing Oliver from wandering, jumping, or using stairs without supervision.

The pen

The exercise pen became Oliver's primary recovery space. I later expanded it to a three-by-three-panel configuration so he had more room to reposition himself while remaining confined.

A partial cover

I used a blanket over part of the pen to make it feel more enclosed and den-like. It gave him some visual shelter without closing off the entire area.

Airflow

I placed a fan outside the pen where Oliver could benefit from the airflow without having access to the fan or its cord.

Water placement

I wanted water close enough that he did not have to move awkwardly. A raised bowl was considered as part of making the space easier for him to use.

BEDS AND COOLING

I prepared soft beds. Oliver kept reminding me that comfort did not always mean softness.

The main pen bed was a large FurHaven orthopedic bed, approximately 53 by 40 by 9.5 inches. Upstairs, Oliver also had a Big Barker bed measuring approximately 48 by 30 by 7 inches. A Kuranda elevated bed was another available option.

Even with those choices, Oliver often preferred cool tile, hard floor, or the space beside the Kuranda bed. At one point, he folded a kitchen rug into his own sleeping surface. We had plenty of dog beds. His behavior made it clear that the bed I considered the best was not always the surface he wanted at that moment.

I stopped treating the recovery area as a finished setup and started treating it as something to observe and adjust. Heat, location, security, habit, and how he felt that day all seemed to affect where he chose to rest. That is an owner observation—not a medical conclusion about which surface is best after TPLO.

Resting options we had available

  • Large FurHaven orthopedic bed in the recovery area
  • Big Barker bed upstairs
  • Kuranda elevated bed
  • Safe access to cooler floor surfaces
  • A fan positioned outside the pen

Exact product models, purchase dates, and long-term performance are still being verified before the product guide is published.

STAIRS AND BATHROOM TRIPS

The physical logistics mattered as much as the pen.

Oliver weighed about 55.8 pounds near the time of surgery. During at least part of recovery, I carried him on the full staircase at night. The two deck steps also required help, especially during nighttime bathroom trips.

This was one of the clearest reminders that a recovery plan has to account for the caregiver's physical reality. “Assist your dog on stairs” sounds simple until the dog is large, the trip is happening in the dark, and you are trying to keep both of you steady.

Oliver's basement bunker created an additional problem. It was his established safe place during fireworks, but reaching it required stairs. I had not fully solved that conflict before surgery, and fireworks later forced me to make difficult, real-time decisions about emotional safety and orthopedic restrictions.

I am not presenting our stair decisions as a model for another dog. The exact stair plan should be discussed with the veterinary team before surgery, especially when a dog has a strong reason to access another floor.

THE CAR

The original injury made safe loading an important part of preparation.

Oliver's injury happened while he was attempting to jump into my 2021 Lexus RX350. That made the vehicle more than a transportation detail—it was part of the problem I needed to solve.

The back seat already had a hammock covering the rear seat and the backs of the front seats. Baxter rode on an elevated bed behind the passenger seat, while Oliver normally used the area behind the driver and the center. That setup helped organize the space once they were inside the vehicle, but the current verified record does not establish exactly which loading method I used after surgery.

Because that detail remains incomplete, I am not turning it into public instructions. A ramp, lifting harness, or two-person method may be appropriate in some situations, but the safest choice depends on the dog, the vehicle, and veterinary guidance.

One thing I would plan earlier: a specific, repeatable way to get the dog into and out of the vehicle before surgery day. The triggering injury involved a car jump, so this should have been a written part of the plan rather than something to improvise later.

WHAT I LEARNED

The most helpful setup was the one I could change.

I would prepare more than one safe resting surface.

Oliver did use orthopedic beds, but he also chose tile, the floor, and a folded rug. Having options was more useful than assuming one expensive bed would solve every comfort problem.

I would build cooling into the plan from the beginning.

The fan and access to cooler surfaces mattered because Oliver repeatedly showed that temperature influenced where he wanted to settle.

I would plan stairs and noise anxiety together.

His safest emotional space was in the basement. His orthopedic restrictions made the basement harder to reach. Those were not separate issues, and I would discuss that conflict with the surgeon before surgery if I were starting over.

I would plan for my own physical limits.

Carrying a large dog, helping on deck steps, managing another dog in the home, and maintaining overnight routines all changed what was realistic.

I would expect the setup to evolve.

The pen was expanded. Beds were moved. A fan and partial cover were added. Oliver's behavior—not the original shopping list—showed me what needed to change.

THE PRACTICAL TAKEAWAY

Prepare the space, then pay attention to the dog living in it.

A recovery area can look perfect and still need to change. The goal was not to create a picture-perfect setup. It was to give Oliver a safe place where he could rest, stay cool, drink, feel included, and move only within the limits we were trying to protect.
