PREPARING THE HOUSE

What should I do before my dog comes home after TPLO surgery?

Preparing the house was one of the few parts of TPLO recovery I could control. I could not make the surgery easier, but I could make the first days at home less chaotic.

This is how I prepared for Oliver. Your dog's discharge instructions and activity limits should come from your veterinarian or surgeon.

THE SHORT ANSWER

Have the basics ready before pickup, but expect the setup to change once your dog is home.

I spent a lot of time trying to build the right recovery space before surgery. That helped. But the most useful setup was not the one I designed in advance. It was the one Oliver gradually showed me he needed.

The goal was never to create a perfect recovery room. It was to make everyday life manageable: sleeping, drinking, going outside, staying cool, feeling secure, and preventing the few movements that could undo all the careful work.

WHAT I WANTED READY BEFORE SURGERY

The first setup solved the obvious problems.

Before Oliver came home, I focused on containment, comfort, water, traction, and how I would move a dog weighing about 56 pounds through the house.

A defined recovery area

I used an exercise pen rather than relying only on a crate. I wanted Oliver to have enough room to lie down, reposition, drink, and rest without wandering through the house.

A large bed

I put a large orthopedic bed inside the pen because I assumed that would be his main recovery surface. It did become useful, but not in the simple way I expected.

Water that was easy to reach

The cone made ordinary bowls awkward. I experimented with placement and height because I did not want drinking to require unnecessary twisting or frustration.

Cooling and airflow

I replaced a loud box fan with a quieter fan and positioned it outside the pen. Oliver repeatedly showed me that airflow mattered to him.

Traction and blocked access

I looked at slippery areas, stairs, landings, furniture, and the routes he would use to go outside. The difficult part was that some of his favorite places were exactly the places recovery made harder to reach.

A plan for nighttime

Oliver normally slept upstairs. I had to think through whether he would stay downstairs, whether I could safely help him on the stairs, and how much changing that routine would affect him.

WHAT I NOTICED AFTER HE CAME HOME

The setup started teaching me where my assumptions were wrong.

On the first day, I worried about almost every detail. Was the tray taking up too much room? Was the bed too large for the pen? Was the bowl positioned correctly? Was he too warm?

Then Oliver started answering some of those questions himself.

He pulled the blankets off the bed. They ended up piled in front of it, and he rested his surgical leg on them. I had not arranged that on purpose. It was a happy accident, but it was also useful information: he was trying to find a position that felt better.

He also chose the space in front of the fan. Later in recovery, he repeatedly chose cool tile, hard flooring, or the area beside a bed instead of the bed itself.

That changed the question I was asking. I stopped asking, “Is this the best recovery bed?” and started asking, “Where does Oliver actually settle?”

THE SETUP EVOLVED

I changed the environment instead of expecting Oliver to fit the original plan.

I added a partial cover

Oliver uses enclosed spaces for security, especially during storms and fireworks. I covered part of the pen while leaving the front open for airflow and visibility.

I expanded the pen

The original configuration felt too tight once the large bed, water, and cone were all part of the space. I made it larger so he could reposition without turning the pen into unrestricted freedom.

I gave him supervised time outside it

Oliver tolerated controlled freedom better than I expected. By the first recovery, the challenge quickly shifted from keeping him confined to preventing specific choices such as getting on the couch.

I preserved participation

Stroller walks, supervised outdoor time, modified ball games, and simply being near the family helped preserve parts of his normal routine without giving him full activity.

THE HARDEST PART TO PLAN

Our house had stairs, and Oliver had strong opinions about where he belonged.

Oliver liked sleeping upstairs, resting on the stair landings, and using the basement bunker during storms. TPLO recovery made every one of those habits harder.

I considered slings, blocking access, carrying him, and changing where he slept. In practice, the plan changed depending on his comfort, the weather, whether he could settle, and what I could physically manage.

This is one area where I would want clearer written instructions before surgery. “Limit stairs” sounds simple until the dog weighs 56 pounds, has a long-established bedtime routine, and becomes distressed when kept away from the one place that helps him feel safe.

Questions I would answer before surgery

  • Where will the dog sleep the first night?
  • How will bathroom trips work?
  • What route has the best traction?
  • What furniture needs to be blocked?
  • How will stairs be managed?
  • Where will food and water go?
  • What happens during storms or fireworks?
  • Who can help if lifting becomes difficult?

WHAT I WOULD DO AGAIN

I would still prepare before surgery day.

Having the pen, bed, water, fan, traction plan, and basic walking path ready made the first hours easier. I was able to focus on Oliver instead of opening boxes and rearranging furniture after he came home.

I would also keep the setup flexible from the beginning. The pen did not need to be perfect. It needed to be easy to change as Oliver showed me what worked.

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY

I would plan around Oliver's routines sooner.

  • Start with a larger recovery area instead of expanding it later
  • Have a better water setup ready before pickup
  • Decide the nighttime and stair plan before surgery
  • Plan for storms and fireworks before they happen
  • Photograph the setup from the beginning
  • Expect the dog to prefer a different surface than the one I bought
  • Think about inclusion, not only restriction

WHAT THIS PAGE CANNOT ANSWER

Your house and your dog will create different problems.

I cannot tell another owner whether a crate, exercise pen, bedroom setup, sling, ramp, or stair plan is medically appropriate for their dog. Those decisions depend on the surgery, the dog's size and behavior, the home's layout, and the veterinary instructions.

What I can say is that the best setup was not the most expensive or the most elaborate. It was the one that solved the real problems in our house and could change as Oliver's needs changed.
