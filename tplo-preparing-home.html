PREPARING THE HOUSE

How do I prepare my house for TPLO recovery?

Start with the way your dog already lives. The best recovery setup is not the one with the most products. It is the one that protects the surgical leg while preserving as much normal life as possible.

This is how I prepared for Oliver. Your dog's discharge instructions and activity limits should come from your veterinarian or surgeon.

THE MOST USEFUL FIRST STEP

Walk through one normal day before you move anything.

Before Oliver's first surgery, I spent a lot of time thinking about pens, beds, bowls, and equipment. Those things mattered, but the more important questions came from his normal routine.

Where did he sleep? How did he get outside? Where did he spend most of the day? Which furniture did he get on without thinking? Where did he go when he was scared? What happened when a package arrived or someone came to the door?

Those answers shaped the setup more than any shopping list could have.

Walk through these moments before surgery

  • Waking up and moving from the sleeping area
  • Going outside for bathroom breaks
  • Eating and drinking while wearing a cone
  • Resting during the day without feeling isolated
  • Reacting to deliveries, visitors, doorbells, or knocking
  • Getting excited at windows, fences, or other regular triggers
  • Using stairs, furniture, and slippery flooring
  • Finding a safe place during storms or fireworks

WHERE I PUT THE RECOVERY PEN

The location mattered as much as the pen itself.

Oliver normally spends part of his day watching what is happening outside, so I positioned the pen where he could still see through the windows. I did not want recovery to take away every familiar part of his day at once.

I also wanted him in a room where he could still be near normal household activity instead of being separated from everything. The goal was containment without making him feel removed from the family.

That choice worked well for ordinary days. The more difficult moments were package deliveries and visitors. A knock, doorbell, or person approaching the house could make Oliver stand up quickly before I had time to redirect him.

If I were preparing again, I would think through those excitement triggers before surgery. The quiet parts of the day were not the hardest part. The sudden moments were.

Before choosing the location

  • Can the dog see familiar household activity?
  • Can the dog rest without being directly beside a busy doorway?
  • Will windows help the dog settle or cause repeated excitement?
  • Can you reach the dog quickly when someone arrives?
  • Is there a safe route to the door and outside?
  • Will the space stay comfortable throughout the day?

WHAT THE LOCATION LOOKED LIKE IN PRACTICE

The pen stayed in the middle of normal household life.

Oliver could still see outside, hear what was happening, and remain close to the family.

The partial cover gave him a more enclosed feeling without removing airflow or visibility. That balance mattered because the room needed to feel familiar without leaving him exposed to every trigger.

ONE DECISION I WOULD MAKE AGAIN

I waited until Oliver was in surgery to set everything up.

I had already planned the layout and purchased what I thought we would need, but I intentionally waited to assemble the recovery area until Oliver was at the hospital.

I did not want him watching me rearrange furniture, build a pen, and change the room before we left. He had no idea surgery was coming, and I wanted his last morning at home to feel as normal as possible.

While he was in surgery, I set up the pen, arranged his bed and water, positioned the fan, and made the space look exactly the way I had pictured it in my head.

By the time I left to pick him up, the house was ready. When we came home, I could focus on Oliver instead of trying to build a recovery space while caring for a dog who had just had major surgery.

WHAT NEEDED TO BE READY

Every item had a job to do.

I did not need a perfect room. I needed a safe place to start, with the few things Oliver would need during the first hours at home.

A defined recovery area

I used an exercise pen because I wanted enough room for Oliver to lie down, reposition, drink, and rest without giving him unrestricted access to the house.

The pen was there to prevent one bad decision when I was not close enough to stop it.

A protected bed

I covered the bed with a waterproof blanket because I was worried he might urinate on it while the anesthesia was still affecting him.

The blanket did its job. Oliver did not pull it off until later, after the anesthesia had worn off and he no longer needed that protection.

Water he could reach with a cone

I knew drinking could become awkward once the cone was on, so I paid attention to the bowl height, placement, and the amount of room around it.

Water should not require unnecessary twisting or repeated frustration.

Cooling and airflow

Oliver runs warm, so I replaced a loud box fan with a quieter fan and positioned it outside the pen where he could benefit from the airflow without reaching the equipment or cord.

He repeatedly chose to lie in front of it, which told me cooling mattered more than I had expected.

A safe path outside

Bathroom trips needed a clear route with as little slipping, jumping, and unnecessary turning as possible.

I looked at flooring, doorways, steps, and the places where excitement could make a slow trip become a sudden movement.

A nighttime plan

Oliver normally slept upstairs. That meant I had to decide where he would sleep, whether he could settle somewhere different, and how stairs would be handled if his normal routine still mattered.

This was not only a mobility question. It was also a routine and comfort question.

THE SETUP CHANGED QUICKLY

What worked on the first day was not necessarily what Oliver needed later.

The waterproof blanket was useful while anesthesia was still a concern. Later, Oliver pulled it off because he no longer needed it covering the bed.

The first pen configuration also became too tight once the large bed, water, and cone all had to fit comfortably. I expanded it so he could reposition more easily without turning the space into unrestricted freedom.

I added a partial cover because enclosed spaces help Oliver feel secure, but I kept the front open for airflow, visibility, and access to the room around him.

None of those changes meant the first setup had failed. They reflected how quickly his needs changed once he was home.

WHY I NEEDED TO SEE THE WHOLE LAYOUT

The bed, cone, water, and remaining floor space all competed for the same room.

From above, it became much easier to see why a setup that looked generous at first could feel tight once Oliver was actually living in it.

The overhead view helped me think about turning space, water access, the bed footprint, and how the covered top changed the sense of enclosure.

THE PART I HAD NOT PLANNED ENOUGH

Normal household excitement became a recovery risk.

Oliver was generally good at resting. The difficult part was not constant movement. It was the sudden moments that made him forget he was recovering.

Packages, visitors, knocking, and the doorbell could make him stand up quickly or move before I had time to intervene. Those events were normal before surgery, so I had not thought of them as part of the recovery plan.

If I were preparing again, I would reduce those surprises where possible. I would use delivery instructions, ask visitors to text instead of knocking, keep the path to the door clear, and make sure I could reach Oliver before opening it.

Recovery preparation is not only about the places your dog rests. It is also about the moments that make them stop resting.

Excitement triggers to plan for

  • Doorbells and knocking
  • Package deliveries
  • Visitors entering the house
  • Other pets reacting to the door
  • Wildlife or activity outside windows
  • Meals, walks, and other routine transitions
  • Storms, fireworks, and other noise triggers

STAIRS, SLEEPING, AND SAFE PLACES

The hardest problems came from routines that already mattered to Oliver.

Oliver liked sleeping upstairs, resting on the stair landings, and using the basement bunker during storms. TPLO recovery made each of those familiar habits harder.

I considered blocking access, using a sling, carrying him, and changing where he slept. In practice, the answer depended on his comfort, the weather, whether he could settle, and what I could safely manage with a dog weighing about 56 pounds.

This is one area where I would want clear written guidance before surgery. Instructions such as “limit stairs” sound simple until the dog's established routine, anxiety, size, and home layout all collide with that restriction.

The useful question is not how many stairs are in the house. It is which important parts of daily life require them—and what the backup plan will be.

WHAT I WOULD DO THE SAME

I would still start with a planned space and keep it flexible.

Having the pen, bed, water, airflow, walking path, and medication instructions ready before pickup made the first hours easier.

I would still position the pen where Oliver could see outside and remain part of normal household life. I would still set it up while he was at the hospital. I would still use a waterproof layer while anesthesia was a concern.

I would also expect the setup to change quickly. The goal is not to predict every need in advance. The goal is to create a safe starting point that is easy to adjust.

IF I HAD ONE AFTERNOON TO PREPARE AGAIN

This is where I would focus my time.

  • Walk through one normal day from the dog's perspective
  • Choose a recovery location that balances visibility, rest, and excitement
  • Plan how bathroom trips, sleeping, food, and water will work
  • Identify furniture, stairs, and flooring that could invite a bad decision
  • Plan for packages, visitors, doorbells, storms, and other sudden triggers
  • Set up the initial space before bringing the dog home
  • Organize medication instructions from the actual prescription labels
  • Expect the first setup to change once the dog shows what works

WHAT THIS PAGE CANNOT ANSWER

Your dog's medical restrictions still come first.

I cannot tell another owner whether a crate, exercise pen, sling, ramp, stair plan, or sleeping arrangement is medically appropriate for their dog.

Those decisions depend on the surgery, the dog's size and behavior, the home layout, and the veterinary instructions.

What Oliver's experience can show is how many ordinary parts of home life become part of recovery, and why the setup needs to work for the dog who will actually use it.
