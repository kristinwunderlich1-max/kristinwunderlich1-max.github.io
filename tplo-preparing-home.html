OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

How should I prepare my home before TPLO surgery?

Prepare the routes, routines, medication system, protection, comfort, and household triggers—not only the pen.

What this page is: Kristin's documented experience with Oliver, separated from veterinary information and remaining uncertainty.

The complete preparation plan I would use now

The goal is not to build a perfect pen. It is to remove predictable problems before your dog comes home: slipping, stairs, jumping, awkward bowls, isolation, medication confusion, and the household triggers that cause sudden movement.

1. Choose the recovery location around daily life.

Oliver needed to rest where he could still feel included. The living room worked because I could supervise him, Baxter could remain nearby, and Oliver did not feel removed from the household. The location also had enough room for the pen to expand after the first version proved too tight.

2. Make every route boring and safe.

Plan the path from the recovery area to the door, the bathroom route, the route to the car, and any unavoidable stairs. Add traction before surgery. Decide where gates or barriers will go. Remove furniture access that is likely to become a negotiation.

3. Prepare for the cone, not just the dog.

The cone changed how Oliver reached food, water, beds, and the sides of the pen. Bowls that looked accessible without it became awkward with it. Test the setup with the cone dimensions in mind and leave enough turning room.

4. Build one medication system from the labels.

Use the actual prescription instructions to create one chart. Keep the medications, chart, pill organizer, and administration supplies in one place. Update the chart when the veterinary team changes the plan. Do not rely on memory when intervals and strengths differ.

Before pickup, confirm

  • Non-slip path to the bathroom area
  • Confinement that allows comfortable turning and rest
  • One supportive bed and one cooler safe option
  • Food and water accessible while wearing the cone
  • Fan and camera cords out of reach
  • Plan for other pets, visitors, deliveries, storms, and fireworks
  • Written medication and emergency-contact information
  • Safe loading and unloading plan for the vehicle

What changed between surgeries

The first recovery taught me that the initial setup would evolve. For the second surgery I started with a larger pen, better airflow, a camera angle that showed Oliver while he slept, and more realistic expectations about how quickly the pen might become a flexible nook rather than a closed enclosure.

I still waited until Oliver was at the hospital to assemble the final setup. That kept his pre-surgery morning normal and meant the room was ready when we returned.
