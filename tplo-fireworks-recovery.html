ANXIETY DURING RECOVERY

Fireworks turned a straightforward recovery plan into a real-life compromise.

Oliver's safest emotional place was his basement bunker. His safest orthopedic plan was to avoid unnecessary stairs. During fireworks season, those two needs collided.

This page describes how I managed Oliver's documented experience. It is not behavioral or medication guidance for another dog.

THE CORE CONFLICT

The place that calmed him was also harder to reach safely.

Oliver had used the basement bunker for years during storms and fireworks. After TPLO, I could not pretend that anxiety disappeared simply because stairs had become inconvenient.

My recovery plan had to account for the whole dog: his knee, his fear, his appetite, his bathroom needs, his medication schedule, and my ability to physically help a dog weighing about 55.8 pounds.

JUNE 27, 2026

A difficult fireworks day

He wanted the basement early

Fireworks began around 1:00 p.m., and Oliver immediately wanted the basement. I allowed him to use the bunker because preventing access to his established safe place could have intensified his panic.

Normal routines became harder

I brought him upstairs for a walk, helped him eat dinner before he returned downstairs, and later brought him up again for medication and a bathroom break.

Eating changed under stress

Oliver would not eat normally later that evening, but he accepted his pills wrapped in cheese. That helped in our home, but it is not presented as universal medication advice.

I was watching for more than fear

That day I also noticed increased limping and licking near his rear area. The source record does not preserve a confirmed explanation or veterinary outcome, so I do not label those observations as a TPLO complication.

WHAT I WAS BALANCING

Every choice affected something else.

  • Restricting stairs while preserving access to Oliver's established safe place
  • Getting him upstairs for food, medication, and bathroom trips
  • Helping him move without increasing panic
  • Watching gait changes without assuming what they meant
  • Deciding when anxiety behavior required veterinary input
  • Managing the physical strain of assisting a large dog

JULY 7, 2026

He went to the bunker before the fireworks started.

By early July, Oliver appeared to anticipate fireworks rather than simply react to the first sound. That changed how I understood his behavior.

He chose the basement around 7:00 p.m.

Oliver went downstairs before I heard fireworks and stayed there much of the night. I brought him up around 10:00 p.m. for medication, and he returned to the bunker.

He eventually transitioned out on his own

Around 1:30 a.m., he came upstairs while Baxter was being let outside. He urinated and defecated, rested in the family room, and later went upstairs to sleep on his Big Barker bed.

Putting himself to bed felt significant

Oliver had not put himself to bed in years. I noticed the moment, but I do not treat it as proof that his anxiety was resolved or that his recovery had reached a particular medical milestone.

The record still has gaps

The exact stair-assistance method, medication details, and full sequence of fireworks that night remain incomplete in the verification record.

WHAT HELPED

Practical choices that reduced conflict

Keeping the bunker available

I did not remove the one place Oliver consistently associated with safety. I adapted the rest of the plan around that reality.

Using cameras

Basement and bedroom cameras let me check where Oliver was resting without repeatedly walking downstairs or disturbing him.

Timing necessary trips

I tried to combine medication, food, and bathroom needs into fewer transitions rather than moving him unnecessarily.

Letting behavior guide the plan

Oliver's choices told me when he needed security, when he was ready to come upstairs, and when he could settle somewhere else.

WHAT I WOULD PLAN EARLIER

I would discuss noise phobia before surgery, not after it became a problem.

If I were planning this recovery again, I would ask the surgeon for a written plan covering stairs, sedation, severe noise events, bathroom trips, and what to do if anxiety made normal confinement unrealistic.

The biggest lesson was not that one choice was always correct. It was that a recovery plan that ignores a dog's known behavioral needs may fall apart when the environment becomes stressful.

WHAT REMAINS UNRESOLVED

I am leaving the uncertain parts uncertain.

  • Exactly how Oliver used the stairs on June 27 and July 7
  • Whether he was carried, assisted, or allowed to walk each trip
  • The complete medication timing and dosage during fireworks
  • The cause and outcome of increased limping
  • The location and meaning of the rear-area licking
  • Whether a veterinarian evaluated those changes
