OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

My dog needs TPLO surgery. Where do I even begin?

Start by preparing for the recovery, not just the surgery. The operation may take a few hours. Recovery will affect how your dog walks, sleeps, eats, goes outside, moves through the house, and participates in everyday life for weeks.

BEFORE YOU DO ANYTHING ELSE

Here are the first things I would do if I were starting over.

I had never heard of TPLO before Oliver needed it. I could find explanations of the procedure, but I had a much harder time finding practical information about living through recovery. After two TPLO surgeries in the same dog, these are the things I would want another owner to know first.

📱

1. Start taking videos now.

Record how your dog walks, stands, sits, gets up, uses stairs, and shifts weight. Oliver often moved differently at home than he did during veterinary appointments. The videos allowed the veterinarian to see exactly what I was seeing when Oliver was not displaying it in the exam room.

A short video can show more than a long explanation, and it gives you something concrete to compare if the pattern changes over time.

🏡

2. Prepare for daily life after surgery.

Walk through an ordinary day in your house. Where does your dog sleep? Which stairs do they use? How do they get outside? What furniture will tempt them to jump? Where can they rest without feeling isolated?

Those questions mattered more in our house than trying to create a perfect-looking recovery area.

🗂️

3. Build one medication system from the actual instructions.

Recovery can involve several medications with different strengths, intervals, start dates, and stop dates. Do not rely on memory or a general schedule you found online.

Use the prescription labels and the instructions from your veterinary team to create one chart that shows what your dog receives, how much, and when. Update it whenever the plan changes.

🧠

4. Plan for the dog you actually have.

Oliver thrives on routine and wants to be part of whatever is happening. He also has significant noise anxiety. Those parts of him did not disappear because his knee needed surgery.

Think ahead about storms, fireworks, visitors, other pets, sleeping routines, and the places your dog goes when they feel anxious. A medically sound plan still has to work in your real home.

🛏️

5. Expect your setup to change.

I planned Oliver's recovery space carefully. Then Oliver showed me what actually worked. The pen changed. The beds changed. The water setup changed. The fan became more important than I expected. Sometimes he preferred a cool floor to an orthopedic bed.

Changing the setup did not mean the original plan failed. It meant I was paying attention.

6. Separate what your dog can do from what has been cleared.

Dogs often feel better before the bone is fully healed. Oliver sometimes showed me that he was physically capable of more than I was allowing, but that did not automatically mean he had been medically cleared for unrestricted activity.

That distinction became especially important with stairs, longer walks, swimming, retrieving, and jumping.

ONE DECISION I WOULD MAKE AGAIN

I waited until Oliver was in surgery to set up the recovery pen.

I had already planned the space and purchased what I thought we would need, but I did not want Oliver watching me move furniture, assemble a pen, and change the room before we left for surgery. I wanted his last morning at home to feel as normal as possible.

While he was at the hospital, I set up the pen, arranged his bed and water, positioned the fan, and made the room look exactly the way I had pictured it in my head.

By the time I left to pick him up, everything was ready. When we came home, I could focus on Oliver instead of trying to build a recovery space while caring for a dog who had just had major surgery.

That approach may not fit every household, but it worked well for us and I would do it the same way again.

What I had ready before pickup

  • A confined recovery area with enough room to rest comfortably
  • A stable sleeping surface and access to a cooler option
  • Water positioned where the cone would not make drinking harder
  • Safe airflow with cords and equipment kept out of reach
  • A clear path for supervised bathroom trips
  • Medication instructions organized before the first dose at home

THE FIRST SETUP IS ONLY A STARTING POINT

Watch your dog more closely than you watch your plan.

Before surgery, I spent a lot of time trying to imagine what Oliver would need. Once he came home, his behavior gave me better information.

He showed me when the pen felt too restrictive. He showed me that airflow mattered. He showed me that the bed I expected him to use would not always be the surface he preferred. He showed me when being included in normal family life helped him settle better than spending all day separated from us.

I stopped asking whether the setup matched the plan in my head and started asking whether it was making recovery safer and easier for Oliver. That question led to most of the useful changes we made.

WHY OLIVER'S EXPERIENCE MAY HELP

He was active, routine-driven, and very good at hiding pain.

Oliver's normal life included daily walks, retrieving, swimming, agility, and dock jumping. He also noticed every change in routine and wanted to be included in whatever was happening.

Before the first diagnosis, hip arthritis seemed like the most likely explanation for what I was seeing. We did not know for certain, but it was the explanation that made the most sense. I took Oliver out of agility for several months to let him rest and switched him to a prescription joint supplement, hoping arthritis was the problem.

Oliver often hid his discomfort during appointments. The videos I took at home allowed the veterinarian to see how he was walking, standing, and sitting when he was not displaying those behaviors in the office.

The knee X-rays ultimately showed the partial right CCL tear and also showed arthritis beginning in his hips. More than one thing was happening, but the knee was the problem that could hopefully be fixed so Oliver could return to his normal life.

Oliver's two surgeries

  • April 16, 2026: Right rear TPLO
  • May 21, 2026: Left rear injury during an attempted jump into the SUV
  • June 5, 2026: Left rear TPLO

Going through one recovery helped with the second, but it did not make the second recovery identical. The same dog can present different challenges each time.

WHAT THIS RESOURCE CAN AND CANNOT DO

This is one owner's documented experience, not a recovery protocol.

Your veterinarian and surgeon should determine your dog's medical restrictions, medication plan, recheck schedule, and return to activity.

What I can share is what everyday recovery looked like in our house: the questions I asked, the problems I had to solve, the things Oliver showed me, what I changed, and what I would do again.

Use our experience to notice something you may have missed, prepare a better question, or think through a part of recovery that is easy to overlook.

WHERE TO BEGIN

Start with the part of recovery that is directly in front of you.

Surgery has not happened yet? Walk through your house and prepare the recovery area. Your dog is already home? Start with the first 72 hours. You do not need to solve the entire recovery today, but you should be able to leave this page with a useful next step.
