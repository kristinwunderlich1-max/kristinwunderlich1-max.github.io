OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

A real-life guide to preparing for TPLO surgery and living through recovery.

Oliver tore the cranial cruciate ligament in his left rear leg in May 2026 and had TPLO surgery on June 5. This resource center shares what the experience was actually like at home: what I worried about, how I prepared, what Oliver used, what changed along the way, and what I learned while helping an active dog recover.

WHY OLIVER'S EXPERIENCE MAY HELP

This was his second TPLO, but it was not simply a repeat of the first.

Oliver is an active Border Collie and Australian Cattle Dog mix who loves walking, swimming, retrieving, agility, and dock jumping. He had already recovered from a TPLO on his other rear leg, so I understood the broad shape of recovery. What I did not know was how much this experience would be shaped by his size, his strong opinions about where he wanted to rest, his fear of fireworks, the stairs to his basement bunker, and my own caution about allowing activity again.

This site does not present Oliver as a typical recovery or a model that another dog should follow. It documents one dog's experience honestly enough that another owner may feel more prepared for the practical decisions that happen outside the veterinary clinic.

RECOVERY AT A GLANCE

The major events that shaped this story.

This is a summary of the strongest dated anchors in the current record. It is not a rehabilitation schedule and should not be used to decide what another dog is ready to do.

  1. May 21, 2026

    The injury

    Oliver hurt his left rear leg while attempting to jump into the car. The limp continued instead of quickly resolving.

  2. May 28, 2026

    The diagnosis

    A veterinarian diagnosed a complete left CCL tear, and surgery planning began.

  3. June 5, 2026

    TPLO surgery

    Oliver underwent left TPLO surgery. The surgeon reported that there was no meniscus damage.

  4. June 5–26, 2026

    Early recovery at home

    Life centered on confinement, bathroom trips, medication routines, monitoring, comfort, sleep surfaces, and gradually increasing use of the operated leg.

  5. June 27 and July 7, 2026

    Fireworks complicated the plan

    Oliver repeatedly wanted his basement bunker, creating a real conflict between his emotional safety and the practical limits of orthopedic recovery.

  6. July 10–13, 2026

    A controlled lake trip

    Oliver participated in the trip while dock jumping was prevented. The trip increased my confidence in his mobility, but it was not proof of medical clearance.

  7. July 16, 2026

    The focus began to change

    At about six weeks after surgery, I began thinking less about protection alone and more about rebuilding muscle, normal movement, and confidence.

WHAT IS NOT BEING PRESENTED AS SETTLED

Some parts of the record still need verification.

The current knowledge package is strong for major dates and direct observations, but several veterinary details are incomplete. Until those records are confirmed, this site will not publish exact medication directions, universal activity timelines, claims about when swimming or stairs are safe, an explanation for Oliver's front-leg tremor, or any statement implying formal six-week healing clearance.

When something feels wrong, contact your veterinarian or an emergency clinic. An owner story can help you prepare questions, but it cannot determine whether your dog's recovery is progressing safely.
