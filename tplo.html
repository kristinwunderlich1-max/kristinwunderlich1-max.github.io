WHY THIS RESOURCE CENTER EXISTS

I could find information about the surgery. I had a harder time finding what everyday life would look like afterward.

Within a few minutes of Oliver’s diagnosis, my mind had moved well beyond the surgery itself.

How was I going to prepare my house? How was I going to keep an active dog from doing something he shouldn’t? What did I actually need to buy before surgery? How would I know if recovery was going well? What if something didn’t seem right?

My veterinarian and surgeon explained the medical side of TPLO very well. What I couldn’t find was much information about living through recovery. I wasn’t looking for another medical opinion. I wanted practical experience from someone who had already done it.

I wanted to know what actually mattered, what turned out not to matter, what worried another owner that ended up being okay, what surprised them, and what they would do differently.

I couldn’t find the resource I was looking for.

I didn’t start documenting Oliver’s recovery because I planned to build a website. I documented it because that’s what I do whenever something important happens. I ask questions. I take pictures. I compare what I’m seeing today with what I saw yesterday. I write things down before I forget them. When something changes, I try to understand why.

It wasn’t until much later that I realized those notes contained exactly the kind of practical information I had been searching for in the first place.

TO UNDERSTAND THE RECOVERY, YOU FIRST NEED TO UNDERSTAND OLIVER

Oliver’s life was built around movement, participation, and routine.

Every day included a walk. Not once in a while. Every day.

Some days it was one mile. Some days it was five. That wasn’t simply exercise. It was part of the rhythm of his life.

He also loved retrieving, swimming, agility, and dock jumping. If something was happening, Oliver wanted to be part of it.

But if I had to describe him with one sentence, it wouldn’t be, “He’s an active dog.” It would be this:

Oliver thrives on routine.

His days have a rhythm. He knows our walking routes. He knows what happens after breakfast. He knows our bedtime routine. He knows when we’re heading to the lake before we even get there. When something changes, he notices—sometimes before I do.

I didn’t fully appreciate how much those routines mattered until surgery disrupted almost all of them at once.

Many of the decisions I made during recovery weren’t really about protecting a healing knee. They were about helping Oliver adapt when the routines that helped him feel secure suddenly disappeared.

WHY OLIVER'S STORY IS A LITTLE DIFFERENT

He went through two TPLO surgeries only weeks apart.

His first TPLO was on his right rear leg on April 16, 2026. While he was still recovering, he injured his left rear leg trying to jump into my SUV. His second TPLO followed on June 5, 2026.

I thought the second recovery would mostly be easier because we had already been through it once. Some parts were. Many weren’t.

Knowing what happened the first time did not tell me exactly what would happen the second time. The circumstances were different. The challenges were different. Some things became easier because I already understood them. Other things surprised me all over again.

Recovery is not a formula. Even with the same dog, every recovery has its own story.

ONE LAST THING

Everything here comes from one dog’s experience.

I’m not trying to tell you what your dog will do. I’m sharing what happened with mine.

Sometimes you’ll read something and immediately recognize your own dog. Sometimes your experience will be completely different. Both are okay.

If something here helps you ask a better question, notice something you might otherwise have missed, or simply reminds you that someone else has been where you are, then sharing Oliver’s experience was worth it.
