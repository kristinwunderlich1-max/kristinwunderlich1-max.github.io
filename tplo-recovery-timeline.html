RECOVERY TIMELINE

What happened, and when?

This page is the dated reference for Oliver's 2026 left CCL injury, TPLO surgery, recovery, and return to increased activity. Use the linked guides when you want the fuller story and practical lessons behind a milestone.

This timeline records Oliver's experience. It is not a recovery schedule for another dog, and it does not replace veterinary instructions.

HOW TO USE THIS PAGE

The dates organize the story. They do not create a universal timetable.

Oliver's recovery was shaped by his injury, surgery, temperament, home, prior activity, and veterinary plan. Another dog may move through the same stages differently.

Each entry below separates the verified event from the larger lesson. Where the current record is incomplete, the uncertainty stays visible.

MAY 21, 2026

The injury happened during an attempted jump into the vehicle.

Oliver began limping after attempting to jump into the vehicle.

Because he already had a history of CCL injury on the opposite leg, the persistent rear-leg limp was more concerning than it would have been before the first diagnosis.

Read how the diagnosis came together →

MAY 28, 2026

The veterinarian diagnosed a complete left CCL tear.

One week after the injury, Oliver was diagnosed with a complete tear of the left cranial cruciate ligament. TPLO was selected, and preoperative planning began.

The current source record does not contain the full examination notes, radiographs, or the surgeon's exact language.

Read the diagnosis journey →

MAY 30–JUNE 4, 2026

The diagnosis became a home and pain-management plan.

Pain management was adjusted, the recovery area was planned, and equipment was gathered. Questions about medication timing, stairs, sleeping, confinement, water, cooling, and transportation all had to be addressed before surgery day.

Preparing the house Products and equipment

JUNE 5, 2026

Oliver underwent left TPLO surgery.

The surgeon reported no meniscus damage. Two shoulder masses were also removed, and biopsy was requested. Oliver came home that evening and began the first-night recovery period.

The exact pickup time, operative report, pathology result, and complete discharge conversation are not verified in the current public source record.

JUNE 5–6, 2026

The first night was about medication, bathroom needs, and learning what Oliver needed.

Oliver had a large initial urination after coming home. At approximately 12:48 a.m., he whined, went outside, and urinated and defecated.

That experience reinforced the importance of checking the whole situation before deciding what one unfamiliar behavior means.

Read the first 72 hours →

JUNE 6–26, 2026

The recovery setup evolved as Oliver used it.

This period included medication, confinement, incision monitoring, bathroom routines, gradual weight-bearing, and repeated changes to beds, water, cooling, crate choices, and available space.

The first setup was a starting point. The later setup reflected what Oliver actually needed.

See how the recovery setup changed →

JUNE 19, 2026

I noticed intermittent shaking in the front right leg.

The shaking appeared while Oliver was partially weight-bearing and resolved when he placed full weight on the leg. I interpreted it as possible compensation, but the current record does not establish a veterinary diagnosis for it.

This is one example of why observations should be preserved without assigning a cause too quickly.

JUNE 27, 2026

Fireworks complicated the recovery plan.

Oliver used his basement bunker extensively because it was his established safe place during fireworks. That created a conflict between emotional safety and the desire to limit stairs.

Food, medication, bathroom needs, movement, and anxiety all had to be managed together.

Read about fireworks and recovery →

JULY 7, 2026

Oliver anticipated fireworks and later chose to sleep upstairs.

He went to the basement before fireworks were clearly audible, remained there for much of the evening, and later came upstairs on his own.

He eventually settled on his upstairs Big Barker bed and put himself to bed, something I had not seen him do in years.

The moment was meaningful, but it was not proof that his anxiety or recovery was resolved.

JULY 10–13, 2026

The lake trip became the first major return-to-life milestone.

Oliver traveled to the lake, participated in controlled activity, retrieved from the beach, and moved through several days that looked more like normal life.

Dock jumping remained off limits. Everyone agreed not to throw from the dock, which removed the strongest cue for the unsafe activity.

Read about returning to normal life →

JULY 16, 2026

The focus began shifting from restriction to rebuilding.

At approximately six weeks after surgery, I let Oliver walk on the regular walk instead of using the stroller.

The lake trip had increased both his confidence and mine. I believed it was time to place more attention on rebuilding activity and muscle while still maintaining the remaining safety boundaries.

That was my interpretation of Oliver's progress, not a general six-week rule.

THE BIG PICTURE

Recovery moved from protection toward participation.

The early phase centered on pain, confinement, medication, bathroom needs, and preventing one unsafe movement.

The middle phase centered on adapting the environment, managing anxiety, and observing how Oliver responded as ordinary life gradually returned.

By mid-July, the question was no longer only how to protect the repair. It was how to rebuild strength and confidence without confusing visible ability with medical clearance.
