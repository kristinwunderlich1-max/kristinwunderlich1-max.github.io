OLIVER'S 2026 LEFT TPLO

A real recovery timeline, with the gaps left visible.

Oliver's recovery did not move in a perfectly straight line. This timeline follows the documented milestones from his injury through the point when I began allowing more walking and focusing on rebuilding strength and confidence.

This is a record of Oliver's experience, not a week-by-week rehabilitation protocol. Your dog's activity limits and recovery plan should come from your veterinary team.

HOW TO READ THIS PAGE

The dates are not medical milestones for every dog.

Some entries are veterinary facts reported at the time. Others are my direct observations, decisions, or later reflections. Where the record is incomplete, I say so rather than filling in the missing details.

MAY 21–JUNE 5, 2026

Injury, diagnosis, and surgery

MAY 21

The injury

Oliver injured his left rear leg while attempting to jump into the car. The limp persisted rather than quickly resolving.

The record does not yet establish the exact mechanics of the jump or whether the ligament was already degenerating before that moment.

MAY 28

A complete left CCL tear was diagnosed

A veterinarian diagnosed a full tear of Oliver's left cranial cruciate ligament. Because he had already experienced TPLO on the opposite leg, I understood the practical burden of surgery and recovery more clearly than I had the first time.

MAY 30–JUNE 4

Pain management and home preparation

I worked through medication timing, confinement, stairs, beds, cooling, and how I would physically manage a dog weighing about 55.8 pounds. The exact medication details are still being reconciled and are not reproduced here.

JUNE 5

Left TPLO surgery

Oliver underwent left TPLO surgery. The surgeon reported no meniscus damage. Two shoulder lumps were also removed, and a biopsy was requested.

The pathology outcome and complete surgical paperwork are not included in the current verified source package.

JUNE 5–JUNE 26

The early recovery period

This is the part of the timeline with the largest documentation gaps. The major moments are preserved, but there is not yet a complete day-by-day record of incision changes, weight bearing, walking distance, appetite, or every medication time.

JUNE 5–6

The first night

Oliver came home, settled into his recovery area, and was monitored closely. He had a large initial urination. At approximately 12:48 a.m., he whined, went outside, and urinated and defecated.

JUNE 6–14

Adjusting the recovery setup

The exercise pen, bedding, fan, partial cover, water placement, and available resting surfaces changed as I watched what Oliver actually preferred. He sometimes chose cool tile or the floor instead of an orthopedic bed.

JUNE 14

A date discrepancy worth preserving

I described Oliver as “12 days out,” but June 14 is nine days after the documented June 5 surgery date. The timeline uses June 5 as the current verified anchor and treats my casual count as an unresolved discrepancy.

JUNE 19

Front-right-leg shaking

I observed Oliver's front right leg trembling under partial weight. The shaking stopped when he placed full weight on it.

I did not preserve a confirmed veterinary explanation, so this page does not label the tremor as compensation, fatigue, pain, or a neurologic problem.

JUNE 27–JULY 7

Fireworks complicated the plan

JUNE 27

The safest emotional place was downstairs

Fireworks drove Oliver toward his basement bunker, creating a real conflict between his anxiety needs and postoperative stair restrictions. I managed food, bathroom trips, medication, and repeated transitions while trying to keep him emotionally and physically safe.

The exact stair-assistance method and the outcome of increased limping and rear-area licking that day still require verification.

JULY 7

He anticipated the fireworks

Oliver went to the basement before hearing fireworks and stayed there much of the night. He later came upstairs, went outside, rested in the family room, and eventually put himself to bed on his Big Barker bed.

That was meaningful because he had not put himself to bed in years, but I do not treat it as proof that his anxiety or recovery was resolved.

JULY 10–JULY 16

The lake trip and the six-week transition

JULY 10–13

A controlled return to a favorite place

Oliver traveled to the lake and participated in beach-based retrieving, walking, visiting, and general activity. He repeatedly returned to the dock because he wanted to jump, but every throw came from the beach and the family followed the same rule.

The amount of swimming, veterinary clearance for water activity, exact distance, and next-day soreness remain incomplete in the record.

JULY 16

My confidence began catching up with his

At approximately six weeks after surgery, I let Oliver walk on the regular walk instead of using the stroller. The lake trip had shown me that he could handle more controlled movement than I had been allowing.

That experience increased my confidence, but it was not the same as formal radiographic healing or unrestricted veterinary clearance.

WHAT THIS TIMELINE TAUGHT ME

Recovery was not only about protecting the knee.

It involved Oliver's anxiety, temperature preferences, sleeping habits, motivation, love of the lake, desire to jump from the dock, and my own fear of allowing too much activity.

The timeline also showed me the difference between what a dog can physically do and what a veterinary team has medically cleared. Oliver's progress helped me see his capacity, but it did not replace formal follow-up care.
