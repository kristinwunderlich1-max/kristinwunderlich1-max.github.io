LESSONS LEARNED

If I had to do TPLO recovery again, what would I do the same—and what would I change?

The biggest lessons were not about buying the perfect product or following one ideal routine. They were about observing closely, preparing for the dog I actually had, adjusting the plan when needed, and learning when protection was helping versus when fear was beginning to hold recovery back.

These are owner reflections from Oliver's recovery. They are not veterinary instructions or a universal TPLO plan.

THE SHORT VERSION

The recovery plan has to protect the leg without erasing the rest of the dog.

Oliver still needed sleep, routine, cooling, familiarity, emotional safety, family time, outdoor time, and eventually more movement.

The strongest parts of our recovery were the parts that protected the surgery while preserving as much normal life as possible.

The lesson I would carry into any future recovery: build the safest reasonable plan, then keep updating it as the dog in front of you changes.

A LESSON FROM THE SECOND INCISION

Do not make “cone back on” depend on memory alone.

Oliver had already been cone-free for a while during his second recovery. On June 12, I forgot to put the cone back on before I stepped away, and he removed a couple of stitches.

The problem was not that supervised cone-free time had been a bad decision. The weak point was the transition between watching him and leaving the room. The cone needed a prominent, dedicated place where I would see it before going upstairs, outside, or leaving the house.

What I would set up now

  • One visible cone station beside the recovery area or exit path
  • A simple rule: cone on before I cross that point
  • No reliance on remembering after I am already distracted
  • Closer monitoring after any new interest in the incision

WHAT I WOULD DO AGAIN

The decisions that proved most useful

Take videos early

Oliver did not always show the same movement at the veterinary clinic that I saw at home.

Videos preserved the gait, sit, weight shift, or hesitation and gave the veterinarian something concrete to review.

I would again record changes instead of relying on memory or hoping the behavior appears during the appointment.

Set up the house while he was at the hospital

I would again plan the recovery area in advance but wait to assemble it until Oliver was in surgery.

That kept his final morning at home normal and meant the house was ready before pickup.

Use a pen, supportive bed, and cone

Those were the three minimum pieces of equipment for Oliver's recovery.

The pen prevented unsafe movement, the bed supported rest, and the cone protected the incision.

Introduce the stroller before surgery

Oliver learned the stroller before he truly needed it.

That meant it was familiar after surgery and allowed him to remain part of walks without covering the full distance.

Keep his safe place available

During storms and fireworks, Oliver still needed the basement bunker.

I would again treat emotional safety as part of orthopedic recovery instead of pretending one need cancelled the other.

Change the people when the dog could not be trusted to change

At the lake, everyone agreed not to throw from the dock.

That worked better than expecting Oliver to resist one of his strongest habits while excited.

WHAT I UNDERESTIMATED

The problems that looked smaller before I lived them

How much space the cone would require

The cone changed Oliver's entire footprint. It affected turning, drinking, sleeping, and how often he hit the pen walls.

I would plan the recovery area around the dog wearing the cone, not the dog standing normally.

How much temperature would matter

Oliver repeatedly chose cool surfaces and airflow.

A supportive bed was useful, but it did not replace his need for a cooler option.

How disruptive ordinary household events could be

Packages, visitors, knocking, and the doorbell could make a calm dog stand or move suddenly.

I would plan for those triggers before surgery rather than treating them as unrelated to recovery.

How complicated stairs would become

“Limit stairs” sounded simple until stairs connected Oliver to sleep, his safe place, and normal routines.

I would ask more specific questions about the full staircase before surgery.

How hard it would be to separate pain, anxiety, and ordinary needs

Whining, panting, restlessness, and movement did not always explain themselves.

The first response had to be checking the whole situation, not choosing a cause too quickly.

How much medication organization would matter

Oliver had several medications at different times, and Baxter had his own schedule.

The pill organizer and one written record became practical safeguards, not optional extras.

ONE NEED I COULD NOT IGNORE

Being physically restricted did not make Oliver less mentally active.

Long periods in the recovery pen removed many of the physical outlets Oliver normally used. That did not remove the energy, focus, or need for engagement behind those habits.

I used simple scent-based enrichment with training cones to give him a job that did not depend on running, jumping, or covering distance.

This was owner-directed enrichment, not a veterinary rehabilitation exercise. I still needed to keep the activity within the movement limits we had been given.

WHAT CHANGED MY THINKING

The dog in front of me mattered more than the plan I imagined beforehand.

The first recovery setup looked reasonable before Oliver used it. Then the cone made the space feel smaller, the water needed to be raised, the box fan proved too loud, and the bed did not solve every comfort need.

None of those changes meant the original plan was careless. They meant the real recovery produced information I could not have had before he came home.

I stopped treating adjustments as evidence that I had done something wrong. They were part of doing the recovery well.

WHAT SURPRISED ME MOST

Oliver was often ready for more before I was.

My caution had a purpose. I did not want one bad movement to create a setback.

But the lake trip showed me that Oliver could handle more ordinary movement than I had been allowing at home. He walked, played, visited, and moved through several days without the obvious setback I feared.

That experience did not become medical clearance. It became evidence that my understanding needed to change.

Recovery required me to protect him and also to notice when fear was beginning to hold him back.

The distinction I would keep

  • What Oliver appeared physically capable of
  • What the veterinary plan had cleared
  • What I was still afraid to allow

WHAT I WOULD CHANGE

The preparation I would make more specific next time

Create one medication record from the start

I would build one chart directly from the current discharge sheet and labels and update it whenever instructions changed.

Get clearer stair instructions

I would ask specifically about climbing, descending, carrying, slings, full staircases, and what to do during severe anxiety.

Plan for storms and fireworks before surgery

I would confirm the safe-space plan, medication timing, cameras, bathroom routine, and backup location in advance.

Track activity more consistently

I would record distance, surface, pace, rest periods, and the next-day response instead of relying on general impressions.

Document every setup change

I would photograph the recovery space each time I changed it and note the problem that prompted the change.

Keep the surgical record complete

I would save the operative report, radiographs, pathology result, exact discharge instructions, and medication labels together.

WHAT I WORRIED ABOUT THAT MATTERED LESS THAN EXPECTED

Not every possible problem became a real problem.

I placed a waterproof blanket on the bed because Oliver had urinated on himself after anesthesia during an earlier procedure. This time, it never happened.

I considered the larger crate important for the lake, and the extra room was helpful, but I am not convinced it was essential.

I kept the Kuranda available, but it was never Oliver's preferred bed.

Preparation still mattered. The lesson is not that those concerns were foolish. It is that some items solve a narrow problem, and sometimes the problem never arrives.

WHAT STAYED USEFUL AFTER STRICT CONFINEMENT

Some recovery purchases kept serving Oliver after the earliest phase ended.

The orthopedic bed remained part of Oliver's normal life after the strictest recovery period was over.

That mattered because the most useful recovery products were not always the ones that solved only one short-term problem. Some continued supporting comfort, routine, and safer movement long afterward.

WHAT I WISH I HAD KNOWN

You do not need to get every decision right before surgery.

You need a safe starting point, clear veterinary instructions, and enough flexibility to respond when the dog shows you something new.

The pen can change. The bed can move. The water setup can be improvised. The walking plan can be updated.

The goal is not to prove that the original plan was perfect. The goal is to keep making the next responsible decision.

IF I HAD TO PREPARE AGAIN TOMORROW

This is where I would focus first.

  • Take baseline videos and photos
  • Confirm the medication, stair, bathroom, and emergency plans in writing
  • Prepare a pen large enough for the dog wearing the cone
  • Provide one supportive bed and one cooler safe surface
  • Make water easy to reach with the cone
  • Reduce package, visitor, doorbell, and window triggers
  • Set up cameras where the dog normally rests
  • Prepare for storms and fireworks
  • Introduce any stroller, crate, or support equipment before it becomes necessary
  • Track activity and the later response, not only the excitement in the moment

WHAT THIS PAGE CANNOT DO

These lessons do not create a universal recovery plan.

Oliver's temperament, size, home, anxiety, prior athletic history, first TPLO experience, and veterinary plan were specific to him.

Another owner may make different choices and still be acting responsibly.

The purpose of these lessons is not to tell someone exactly what to do. It is to show the decisions that mattered, the assumptions that changed, and the questions worth asking earlier.
