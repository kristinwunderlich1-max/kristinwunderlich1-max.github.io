OLIVER'S TPLO RESOURCE CENTER

What did I learn after living through TPLO recovery twice?

The strongest lessons were about systems, inclusion, temperature, anxiety, memory, and the difference between capacity and clearance.

What this page is: Kristin's documented experience with Oliver, separated from veterinary information and remaining uncertainty.

The lessons I would want beside me before surgery

These are not universal recovery rules. They are the practical conclusions that remained after living through two surgeries with the same dog.

Prepare a system, not a perfect pen.

The room, routes, medication chart, cone location, cooling, cameras, and other-dog plan mattered together. The enclosure itself was only one part.

Keep the dog included without confusing inclusion with freedom.

Stroller walks, supervised lounging, outdoor observation, and modified play protected Oliver's routines without giving him unrestricted activity.

Temperature can explain behavior that looks like rejection.

Oliver leaving a bed did not always mean the bed was wrong. He often wanted airflow or a cooler surface.

Do not rely on memory for protective equipment.

The second incision problem happened because the cone had been off during supervised time and I forgot to replace it before stepping away. A visible cone station would have prevented the transition from depending on memory.

Plan for anxiety before the weather arrives.

Fireworks affected eating, medication, stairs, confinement, and sleep. The orthopedic plan had to include Oliver's established basement refuge.

Capacity, confidence, and clearance are different.

The lake showed Oliver could tolerate more activity than I expected and helped both of us regain confidence. That did not replace imaging or veterinary guidance.

Mistakes I would openly admit

  • I initially tried to make the recovery setup fit my plan instead of expecting it to evolve.
  • I underestimated how much temperature would influence where Oliver rested.
  • I let the cone transition depend on memory and Oliver removed stitches.
  • I did not have a complete storm-and-stairs plan before the problem arrived.
  • I sometimes treated a good day as a test instead of looking carefully at the next-day response.

What surprised me most

Oliver regained household freedom quickly during the first recovery, yet the second recovery created more incision vigilance. The same dog did not repeat the same recovery. Previous experience helped me adapt faster, but it did not eliminate uncertainty.
