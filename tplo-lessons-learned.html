LOOKING BACK

The lessons Oliver taught me that I couldn't have learned from a discharge sheet.

Some lessons came from things that worked. Others came from situations I never expected to face. Together they changed how I think about orthopedic recovery.

These reflections are based on Oliver's experience and are not medical recommendations.

LESSON ONE

Recovery is about the whole dog, not just the knee.

Oliver's recovery involved his anxiety, sleeping habits, preferred temperatures, favorite places, motivation to retrieve, and desire to stay involved with family life. The knee was only one part of the recovery plan.

WHAT SURPRISED ME

The biggest lessons were practical.

Confidence is part of rehabilitation

I expected muscle rebuilding to be important. I did not expect my own confidence to need rebuilding too.

Observe before buying more

Oliver often chose cool tile or the floor instead of expensive beds. Watching him taught me more than shopping did.

Environment beats willpower

Changing where people threw the ball at the lake was more effective than expecting Oliver to resist the dock on his own.

Preparation reduces stress

Having the recovery area ready before surgery meant I could focus on Oliver instead of assembling equipment after bringing him home.

Cameras became part of caregiving

Monitoring Oliver remotely let me observe natural behavior instead of interrupting his rest every few minutes.

Fireworks change everything

A recovery plan that ignores an existing behavioral challenge may not survive the first major stressor.

WHAT I WOULD CHANGE

If I were starting over

  • Create one master medication sheet directly from prescription labels.
  • Photograph every stage of recovery.
  • Track walking distance and weight bearing in one place.
  • Discuss fireworks and stair planning before surgery.
  • Document products immediately after purchasing them.
  • Remember that one successful day does not replace veterinary clearance.

None of these ideas change the outcome of Oliver's surgery. They simply would have made the experience easier to manage and easier to document accurately.

THE BIGGEST TAKEAWAY

Perfect recovery wasn't the goal. Thoughtful recovery was.

I made decisions using the information I had at the time. Looking back, I can see things I would improve, but I don't judge those earlier decisions by information I didn't yet have.

That perspective matters because every dog owner eventually finds themselves making decisions without certainty.

WHAT COMES NEXT

The complete TPLO Resource Center brings these lessons together.

The individual stories explain specific moments. Together they become a practical roadmap that I hope helps another owner feel more prepared and less alone.
