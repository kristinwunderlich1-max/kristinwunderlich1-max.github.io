# Case File #001: Oliver's TPLO Journey
## Master Knowledge Record — Version 1.0
**Status:** Working source document  
**Purpose:** This is the authoritative record behind the website’s TPLO recovery center. It is not medical advice and is not intended to be published as-is.

## 1. Oliver at a Glance

- **Name:** Oliver
- **Date of birth:** July 22, 2015
- **Breed mix:** 46.5% Australian Cattle Dog, 32.6% Border Collie, 5.9% Collie, 5.3% German Shepherd Dog, 9.7% Supermutt
- **Weight near surgery:** 55.8 pounds
- **Activity before injury:** Active; enjoys walking, swimming, retrieving, agility, and jumping from the dock
- **Relevant orthopedic history:** Previous TPLO on the right rear leg for a partial CCL tear
- **Current case:** Full CCL tear of the left rear leg, treated with TPLO on June 5, 2026
- **Meniscus:** No meniscus damage found during surgery
- **Other relevant traits:** Noise-sensitive, especially with thunderstorms and fireworks; uses a basement “bunker” as a safe place

### Why this context matters

Oliver was not a sedentary dog learning to become active. He was an active, confident dog being asked to tolerate weeks of restriction. His previous orthopedic history, age, size, anxiety, home layout, and love of water all shaped the decisions made during recovery.

---

## 2. The Injury

### May 21, 2026 — Injury event

Oliver attempted to jump into the car and began limping afterward.

### What was observed

- Persistent limping
- Reduced use of the left rear leg
- Concern that the injury was more than a temporary strain
- The limp did not resolve with time and rest

### What I initially had to figure out

- Whether this was soreness, a strain, or another cruciate injury
- How much activity to allow while waiting for the veterinary evaluation
- How to manage pain and movement safely
- Whether his prior TPLO made a second cruciate injury more likely or more complicated

### Early lesson

A single awkward jump can be the obvious moment an existing ligament problem becomes visible. The injury event is important, but it may not tell the entire story of how or when the ligament weakened.

---

## 3. Diagnosis and Decision

### May 28, 2026 — Diagnosis

Oliver was diagnosed with a **full left CCL tear**.

### Decision

TPLO surgery was selected.

### Factors that supported the decision

- Oliver’s size
- His active lifestyle
- The full nature of the tear
- Prior experience with TPLO on the opposite leg
- The goal of giving him the best chance to return to walking, swimming, and normal activity

### Emotional reality

Even with prior TPLO experience, the second surgery was not emotionally easy. Knowing what recovery involves can reduce uncertainty, but it can also make the owner very aware of how difficult the first days and weeks may be.

---

## 4. Pre-Surgery Pain Management

Before surgery, Oliver’s pain and anxiety required active management.

### Medications used or discussed before surgery

- **Gabapentin:** increased to every eight hours because of panting and discomfort
- **Carprofen:** every twelve hours
- **Trazodone:** 100 mg, 1.5 tablets every twelve hours
- **Acepromazine:** available as needed, but not used together with trazodone

### Practical scheduling reality

A medication plan must work in the actual household. A 2:00 a.m. dose was acceptable in this home because it was more realistic than forcing the entire schedule into conventional daytime hours.

### Lesson

The “best” medication schedule is not only pharmacological. It must also be one the owner can follow consistently without guessing or missing doses.

---

## 5. Preparing the Home

Oliver’s recovery environment evolved instead of remaining fixed.

### Primary recovery area

- Exercise pen expanded to approximately 3-by-3 panels
- Large Furhaven orthopedic bed: approximately 53 x 40 x 9.5 inches
- Fan positioned outside the pen
- Blanket used overhead to create a calmer, den-like environment
- Raised water bowl requested
- Big Barker bed upstairs: approximately 48 x 30 x 7 inches
- Kuranda bed added later because Oliver repeatedly chose cooler, firmer surfaces

### What Oliver taught me about beds

Oliver did not always use the bed that seemed objectively “best.” He often selected:

- Tile because it was cooler
- The couch because it was familiar and comfortable
- A rug he folded himself
- The floor beside a newly placed bed
- His usual preferred locations rather than the most expensive orthopedic option

### Important product lesson

Buying a high-quality bed does not guarantee the dog will use it. Temperature, location, familiarity, firmness, and access may matter more than price.

### Stairs

- Oliver was carried on the full staircase at night during early recovery
- He received help with the two deck steps, especially at night
- Stair management was physically demanding because Oliver weighed nearly 56 pounds

### Flooring and movement

The home setup needed to account for slippery tile, safe routes to the yard, limited stairs, controlled potty trips, and keeping Oliver near the family without allowing uncontrolled movement.

## 6. Travel and Vehicle Setup

### Vehicle

2021 Lexus RX350

### Normal travel arrangement

- Full back-seat hammock covering the seat and front-seat backs
- Baxter elevated behind the passenger seat
- Oliver behind the driver and across the center
- Oliver often watches from the center

### Lake crate decision

A 42-inch crate was considered and ordered for lake use because Oliver already understood crates, fireworks were possible, and an exercise pen might not feel secure enough during a fear response. Cooling orthopedic crate mats were ordered and shipped to a lake contact.

### Decision-journal entry

**Decision:** Use a larger crate rather than relying only on an exercise pen for travel.  
**Why:** Oliver’s firework anxiety made secure containment more important than maximizing open floor space.  
**Would I make the same decision again?** Likely yes, especially for an anxious dog recovering away from home.

## 7. Surgery Day

### June 5, 2026 — Left TPLO

- Left TPLO completed
- No meniscus damage
- Two shoulder lumps removed during the same procedure
- Biopsy requested

### Discharge medications

- **Cephalexin chew 150 mg:** half chew beginning the evening of June 5 for two weeks
- **Cephallexin 500 mg:** every twelve hours for two weeks
- **Carprofen 100 mg:** half tablet every twelve hours for two weeks, beginning June 6 at noon
- **Acepromazine 25 mg:** every eight to twelve hours as needed
- **Gabapentin:** continued

### First evening

Oliver received gabapentin, acepromazine, and cephalexin.

### First-night events

- Large initial urination
- At approximately 12:48 a.m., Oliver whined
- He was taken outside and urinated and defecated

### First-night lesson

Whining was meaningful communication. It was not automatically pain, panic, or medication reaction. In this case, he needed to go outside.

---

## 8. Medication Management

### Why this was difficult

Oliver had multiple medications with different frequencies, start dates, interactions, and purposes.

### What helped

- Building the schedule around actual wake and sleep patterns
- Allowing a 2:00 a.m. dose when necessary
- Wrapping medication in cheese when appetite or willingness was low
- Bringing medicine to Oliver rather than forcing him to relocate during anxious periods

### Lesson

Medication administration is not separate from behavior and environment. Fear, appetite, mobility, and location all affect whether a plan is workable.

---

## 9. Early Recovery Environment and Behavior

Oliver frequently preferred tile and other cooler surfaces. A fan helped, but bed selection remained his choice.

Despite having many dog beds, Oliver sometimes chose the couch, lay beside a newly placed Kuranda bed, folded a rug into a makeshift bed, or returned to familiar locations rather than the designated recovery bed.

### Practical rule worth publishing

Provide multiple safe surface options when possible:

- Soft orthopedic
- Firmer elevated
- Cooler floor-level surface
- Familiar resting location

The dog’s actual behavior is data.

## 10. Fireworks During TPLO Recovery

Noise anxiety complicated orthopedic recovery.

### June 27, 2026

- Fireworks began around 1:00 p.m.
- Oliver went to the basement
- He was brought upstairs around 6:00 p.m. for a walk
- More fireworks occurred after the walk
- He ate dinner before returning downstairs
- Around 10:00 p.m., he was brought up for medication
- He urinated quickly and would not eat normally
- Pills were given in cheese
- Acepromazine was given
- He returned to the basement
- He came upstairs around midnight and later slept

### July 7, 2026

- Oliver went to the basement around 7:00 p.m. before hearing fireworks because he expected them
- He remained there most of the night
- He was brought out around 10:00 p.m. for medicine
- He returned to his bunker
- Around 1:30 a.m., he came upstairs, urinated, defecated, and rested in the family room
- Around 2:50 a.m., he went upstairs and put himself to bed on the Big Barker
- This was notable because he had not put himself to bed in years

### Major lesson

Recovery plans must account for the whole dog. Orthopedic restrictions do not erase fear, habits, or emotional needs.

### What worked

- Allowing access to the basement bunker
- Using cameras to monitor him
- Bringing medications to him
- Using cheese when he would not eat
- Letting him choose when he was ready to rejoin the household

## 11. Mobility, Weight Bearing, and Muscle Changes

### Front right leg tremor — June 19

Oliver’s front right leg shook when he put only slight pressure on it.

Observed pattern:

- Happened while standing still
- Could happen while sitting or lying down
- Resolved when he placed full pressure on the leg
- Did not appear painful
- He did not inspect or lick the leg
- It resembled muscle fatigue

### Interpretation at the time

Possible compensation fatigue from shifting weight away from the recovering rear leg.

### Lesson

Recovery affects the entire body. Owners often focus only on the surgical leg, but front limbs, shoulders, back, and the opposite rear leg may all work differently during compensation.

## 12. Six-Week Milestone and Return to Walking

### July 16, 2026 — Approximately six weeks post-surgery

Oliver walked on the regular walk instead of using the stroller.

### Why the decision changed

The lake trip showed that Oliver could comfortably accumulate substantial movement, appeared confident, and was physically capable of more than had been allowed at home. The trip increased both Oliver’s confidence and my confidence.

### Decision-journal entry

**Decision:** Begin allowing more walking and intentional muscle rebuilding.  
**Information available:** Six weeks had passed since surgery, Oliver had tolerated significant activity at the lake, and he appeared ready for more.  
**Reasoning:** Continuing to restrict him too heavily could slow muscle rebuilding and confidence.  
**What must remain clear on the website:** Oliver’s progression was specific to his surgical follow-up, condition, and behavior. Another owner should use their surgeon’s restrictions rather than copy his mileage.

---

## 13. Lake Trip and Water-Based Activity

### July 10–13, 2026

- Oliver moved extensively through general activity, visiting, and play
- He repeatedly wanted to return to the dock
- He clearly wanted to jump into the water
- Everyone respected the restriction and did not throw from the dock for him
- Throws were made from the beach instead
- He demonstrated confidence and strong interest in normal activities

### Important emotional milestone

The trip helped restore Oliver’s confidence in his body, my confidence in his recovery, and a sense of normal life.

### What worked

- Setting a clear rule that nobody should encourage dock jumping
- Having family and friends follow the restriction
- Redirecting retrieving to the beach
- Allowing controlled participation rather than excluding him completely

### Potential mistake or risk to examine honestly

The total amount of accumulated activity may have exceeded what a formal week-six rehabilitation plan would recommend. The website should be honest about that rather than presenting the lake trip as a universal protocol.

### Lesson

A dog can look capable before tissues are fully healed. Confidence and enthusiasm are useful information, but they are not the same as surgical clearance.

## 15. What Helped Most

- Prior TPLO experience
- A flexible medication schedule
- Multiple safe resting surfaces
- A larger recovery pen
- Camera monitoring
- A safe basement bunker for fireworks
- Family and friends respecting activity restrictions
- Redirecting lake play away from dock jumping
- Observing Oliver rather than relying only on a predetermined setup
- Gradually allowing more movement when his recovery supported it
- Using food he would reliably accept for medication
- Preserving normal routines wherever safely possible

---

## 16. What Did Not Work as Expected

- Assuming Oliver would use the bed placed where he had been lying
- Assuming the most expensive or orthopedic surface would always be preferred
- Treating the recovery environment as static instead of adapting it
- Expecting anxiety-related routines to pause during orthopedic recovery
- Using only formal milestones without considering confidence and muscle loss
- Potentially allowing too much accumulated lake activity because Oliver looked capable
- The challenge of a 36-inch crate for a nearly 56-pound dog during longer confinement
- The difficulty of separating normal owner worry from genuine warning signs

## 18. What I Would Do Again

- Prepare multiple resting surfaces
- Keep the recovery space near normal household activity
- Use cameras for monitoring without repeatedly disturbing him
- Build medication times around a schedule I can actually maintain
- Keep a secure anxiety-safe space available
- Tell everyone clearly what activities are prohibited
- Redirect desired activities rather than eliminating all participation
- Track exact dates and milestones
- Take photos and videos during recovery
- Pay attention to confidence as well as gait
- Ask for a realistic post-six-week muscle rebuilding plan
- Treat behavior, anxiety, and mobility as one integrated recovery

## 19. Questions for the Surgeon or Veterinarian

### Before surgery

- What medications should stop before surgery?
- What should I give the morning of surgery?
- What type of containment is required?
- How should I manage stairs?
- What symptoms would require urgent evaluation?
- What rehabilitation do you recommend and when should it begin?
- What are the specific restrictions for swimming, jumping, and longer walks?

### At discharge

- Exactly when does each medication start?
- Which medications may be given together?
- Which sedatives should not be combined?
- What should eating, urination, and defecation look like in the first 24–72 hours?
- What amount of swelling, bruising, warmth, or discharge is expected?
- When should I send a photo or call?

### During recovery

- Is this amount of limping expected at this stage?
- Could compensatory front-leg shaking reflect fatigue?
- When should walking duration increase?
- When can formal strengthening begin?
- When is swimming permitted?
- How should setbacks be handled?
- What does radiographic healing need to show before unrestricted activity?

## 21. Key Lessons — Draft

1. Recovery is not linear.
2. The surgical leg is not the only body part affected.
3. A dog’s emotional needs do not pause during orthopedic recovery.
4. The most expensive bed may not be the one the dog chooses.
5. A medication schedule must fit real life.
6. Clear rules for family and visitors matter.
7. Confidence can return before full biological healing.
8. Controlled normalcy may be more sustainable than total exclusion.
9. Photos, videos, and dated notes are extremely valuable.
10. Honest reflection about over-restriction and overactivity is more helpful than pretending recovery was perfect.

## 25. Letter to the Next Dog Owner — Draft

If you are reading this because your dog is about to have TPLO surgery, I know how overwhelming it can feel.

Even though Oliver had already been through a TPLO on his other leg, I still worried about the medications, the stairs, the first night, every limp, every unusual movement, and whether I was allowing too much or too little. I bought things I thought would be perfect that he barely used. I changed the setup when his behavior showed me I had guessed wrong. I sometimes worried more than I needed to, and there were also moments when his enthusiasm made it easy to forget that feeling better was not the same as being fully healed.

This record is not a perfect recovery story. It is the honest version.

I want you to see what helped, what did not, what scared me, what I would repeat, and what I would change. My hope is not that you copy every decision I made. It is that you begin your own dog’s recovery with more questions answered, more options to consider, and a better understanding of what everyday life after TPLO can actually look like.

Work closely with your veterinarian and surgeon. Pay attention to your dog. Keep notes. Ask questions. Adapt when something is not working.

You do not have to do everything perfectly to care for your dog well.
