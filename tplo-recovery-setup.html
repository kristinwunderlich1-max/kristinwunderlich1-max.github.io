RECOVERY SETUP

How do I know whether my dog's TPLO recovery setup is actually working?

A recovery setup is working when it safely limits the movements you are trying to prevent, allows your dog to rest and meet basic needs, and can be adjusted as those needs change. Oliver's final setup was not the one I pictured before surgery. It was the one we arrived at after I watched how he actually used the space.

This is Oliver's personal experience. Follow your own veterinarian's instructions for confinement, flooring, stairs, activity, incision protection, and return to normal movement.

THE SHORT ANSWER

Stop judging the setup by how it looks. Judge it by what your dog can safely do in it.

Before surgery, I wanted the recovery area to be complete. Once Oliver came home, I realized that “complete” was the wrong goal.

The pen had to contain him without making it hard to turn around. The bed had to support him, but it also had to leave room for water and movement. The fan had to help without creating a cord hazard. The location had to let him see outside and stay part of normal life without placing him directly in the path of every delivery or visitor.

The setup worked because it could change. I was not trying to preserve the original plan. I was trying to solve the next real problem.

Signs the setup may need to change

  • Your dog cannot reposition without bumping into the bed, bowl, cone, or pen
  • Your dog repeatedly chooses the floor, doorway, or another location instead of the bed
  • The water bowl is difficult to reach while wearing a cone
  • Your dog cannot settle because the area is too warm, exposed, isolated, or busy
  • Normal household triggers repeatedly cause sudden standing or movement
  • You cannot safely reach the dog or move them outside when needed
  • The setup works only when someone is watching every second

THE EXERCISE PEN

The pen was there to prevent one bad decision—not to make Oliver live in the smallest possible space.

I chose an exercise pen instead of relying only on a crate because I wanted Oliver to have enough room to lie down, change positions, drink, and rest without wandering through the house.

The first configuration became too tight once the large bed, water, and cone all had to fit inside. The setup technically contained him, but it did not give him enough room to use the space comfortably.

I expanded the pen to a larger configuration. That gave him more room without giving him unrestricted access to the house.

That change was not about making recovery less strict. It was about making the restriction realistic enough that Oliver could actually live in it.

What I needed the pen to allow

  • Standing and lying down without awkward twisting
  • Changing positions without climbing over bedding
  • Reaching water while wearing a cone
  • Resting without being isolated from normal household life
  • Safe access for medication and bathroom assistance
  • Enough containment to prevent furniture, stairs, and wandering

THE BED WAS NOT THE WHOLE ANSWER

I had good orthopedic beds. Oliver still chose other surfaces.

Oliver had access to a large Furhaven orthopedic bed in the recovery pen and a Big Barker bed upstairs. Both were useful. Neither became the only place he wanted to rest.

Sometimes he chose tile or another hard, cool surface. Later, when I placed a Kuranda bed where he had been lying, he sometimes rested beside it instead of on it.

At first, it was tempting to assume that choosing the floor meant the bed was wrong or he was uncomfortable. The pattern made more sense once I considered temperature. Oliver often preferred the cooler option even when several soft beds were available.

The useful question was not, “Did I buy the best bed?” It was, “Where can he settle safely, and what does he keep choosing?”

WHAT OLIVER'S CHOICES CHANGED

Comfort was a combination of support, temperature, security, and routine.

The fan became essential

I replaced a loud box fan with a quieter fan and placed it outside the pen where Oliver could benefit from the airflow without reaching the equipment or cord.

He repeatedly chose to lie in front of it. That made it clear that staying cool was part of comfort, not an extra feature.

The waterproof blanket was temporary

I placed it over the bed because I was worried about urination while anesthesia was still affecting him.

It did its job. Oliver did not pull it off until later, after the anesthesia had worn off and he no longer needed that layer.

The partial cover helped with security

Oliver uses enclosed spaces when he wants to feel safe, so I covered part of the pen while leaving the front open for airflow, visibility, and access.

I was trying to give him some shelter without turning the recovery area into a closed box.

The water setup needed room

The cone changed how much space Oliver needed around the bowl. Placement that looked fine without the cone could become awkward once he was wearing it.

I adjusted the height and location so drinking did not require unnecessary twisting or frustration.

The view outside helped preserve routine

Oliver normally spends part of the day watching the neighborhood, so I positioned the pen where he could still see outside.

That familiar activity mattered, although packages and visitors also made the location more exciting than I had fully anticipated.

The best surface changed over time

The bed, blanket, tile, hard floor, and elevated bed each served a different purpose at different moments.

I stopped expecting one surface to solve every comfort problem for the entire recovery.

CONFINEMENT WAS NOT THE ONLY TOOL

As Oliver progressed, the pen became one part of the plan instead of the whole plan.

During the first recovery, Oliver began joining walks in the stroller by Day 5 and had supervised time outside the pen by Day 6. By approximately Day 10, he had enough main-floor freedom that keeping him off the couch had become a bigger problem than keeping him inside the pen.

That did not mean unrestricted activity was safe. It meant Oliver was generally willing to settle when he had controlled freedom, and the risk came from a few specific choices: furniture, stairs, sudden excitement, and trying to resume normal routines too quickly.

The pen remained useful when I could not supervise closely, when he needed protection from a bad decision, and during the earliest part of recovery.

The lesson for me was not that every dog should have early freedom. It was that confinement should solve a specific safety problem, and the veterinary plan still determines when changes are appropriate.

What supervised freedom still required

  • No access to stairs or furniture unless specifically managed
  • No chasing, jumping, running, or sudden pivots
  • A quiet environment with predictable movement
  • Someone close enough to interrupt a risky choice
  • A return to confinement when supervision was not possible
  • Activity kept within veterinary instructions

THE PROBLEM WAS OFTEN SUDDEN EXCITEMENT

A calm dog can still make one fast decision.

Oliver was generally good at resting. The harder moments came when something interrupted that calm.

A package delivery, visitor, knock, doorbell, sound outside, or another dog reacting could make him stand quickly before I had time to redirect him.

That is why the recovery setup had to include more than the pen itself. I also had to think about delivery instructions, visitors texting instead of knocking, who would answer the door, and how close I could be when a predictable trigger happened.

A recovery area can be physically secure and still sit inside an environment full of sudden reasons to move.

CAMERAS BECAME PART OF THE SETUP

Monitoring helped me observe without repeatedly disturbing him.

I already had cameras in several parts of the house. During recovery, I added coverage for Oliver's basement bunker, the larger basement area, and the bedroom.

The cameras let me check whether he was sleeping, pacing, licking, changing positions, or moving between areas without walking into the room every time.

That was especially useful when fireworks drove him to the basement for long periods and when I wanted to understand what happened overnight.

The cameras did not tell me whether a behavior was medically concerning. They gave me a more complete record of what Oliver was actually doing.

Useful camera questions

  • Can you see the full resting area rather than only one corner?
  • Can you see the route to water and the exit?
  • Are there blind spots where licking or pacing could be missed?
  • Will lighting still work at night?
  • Can you review saved clips instead of relying only on live viewing?
  • Is the camera helping you observe rather than making you check constantly?

WHAT I WOULD DO AGAIN

I would build a safe starting point and expect Oliver to help refine it.

I would still use an exercise pen, place it where Oliver could see outside and remain part of household life, provide more than one safe resting surface, use a fan, and keep water accessible with the cone.

I would still use cameras and supervised normal moments as the veterinary plan allowed.

Most importantly, I would still change the setup when Oliver repeatedly showed me that something else worked better.

WHAT I WOULD CHANGE

I would plan for flexibility from the beginning.

  • Start with enough room for the bed, water, cone, and safe repositioning
  • Provide a cooler surface in addition to a soft bed
  • Position the pen for visibility without placing it directly beside every trigger
  • Have delivery and visitor plans ready before surgery
  • Make the water setup easy to adjust
  • Include a safe airflow plan from Day 1
  • Photograph each setup change and note why it was made
  • Decide what supervised freedom would require before it becomes an issue

WHAT THIS PAGE CANNOT ANSWER

The safest level of confinement is a medical decision.

Oliver's ability to settle with controlled freedom does not mean another dog should follow the same timeline. A different dog may be more active, more anxious, less steady, or under different surgical restrictions.

Use this page to think through comfort, routine, triggers, and practical setup problems. Use your veterinarian or surgeon to decide what movement and freedom are medically appropriate.
