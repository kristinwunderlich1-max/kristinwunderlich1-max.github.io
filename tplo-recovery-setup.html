RECOVERY ENVIRONMENT

Building a recovery space that worked for Oliver—not the one I imagined.

Before surgery I focused on creating a safe environment. During recovery I discovered that Oliver kept teaching me how to improve it. The setup evolved as I watched what he actually preferred.

This page describes my home setup for Oliver. It is not a universal recovery plan or veterinary recommendation.

THE GOAL

Comfort, safety, and realistic daily living

I wasn't trying to build the perfect recovery room. I was trying to create a space where Oliver could rest safely while still fitting into our normal life.

Exercise pen

I chose an exercise pen instead of relying only on a crate. It gave Oliver more room while still preventing uncontrolled movement. As recovery continued, I expanded it into a larger configuration because his behavior showed he could benefit from the extra space.

Several places to rest

Oliver had access to a FurHaven orthopedic bed, his Big Barker bed upstairs, a Kuranda bed, cool tile, and other safe resting spots. One lesson became obvious: the bed I thought he would choose was not always the one he actually wanted.

Cooling mattered

A fan outside the pen, access to cooler flooring, and multiple sleeping surfaces helped me adapt the environment to Oliver instead of expecting Oliver to adapt to the environment.

Water within easy reach

I positioned water close to his recovery area and explored a raised bowl to reduce awkward movement during recovery.

REAL-WORLD CHALLENGES

The recovery plan had to fit our house

Stairs

Oliver weighed about 55.8 pounds. Carrying him up and down stairs became one of the hardest physical parts of recovery. His love of the basement during fireworks made those decisions even more complicated.

Camera monitoring

I expanded my camera coverage to include Oliver's basement bunker, other basement resting areas, and the bedroom. The cameras let me observe him without constantly disturbing his rest.

Vehicle setup

Because the injury happened during an attempted jump into my Lexus RX350, safe vehicle loading became part of recovery planning. My existing hammock setup remained useful, but loading strategy became much more important.

Observe first, buy second

One of the biggest lessons was to watch Oliver before buying more equipment. His behavior often showed me what actually needed to change.

WHAT I WOULD DO AGAIN

Build the environment before surgery day.

Having the pen, beds, cooling, and basic layout ready before pickup reduced stress considerably. After Oliver came home, I could focus on him instead of assembling equipment.

I would also photograph every stage of the setup. Those photos became valuable evidence later when reconstructing the story.
