REBUILDING STRENGTH

When protecting Oliver became letting him move again.

The hardest part of recovery eventually stopped being keeping Oliver quiet. It became deciding when my caution was holding him back more than helping him.

This page documents Oliver's recovery experience. It is not a rehabilitation schedule.

THE TURNING POINT

My confidence lagged behind Oliver's.

Early in recovery I relied on a stroller so Oliver could still be included without walking too far. After our July lake trip, I realized he was capable of more controlled activity than I had been allowing.

On July 16, about six weeks after surgery, I let him complete our regular walk instead of riding in the stroller. That decision reflected my growing confidence in him—not formal veterinary clearance.

WHAT CHANGED

Recovery became rebuilding.

The stroller had done its job

It reduced walking while still allowing Oliver to participate in outings. As his recovery progressed, I gradually depended on it less.

The lake changed my perspective

Watching Oliver walk, visit, retrieve from the beach, and remain engaged gave me confidence that carefully increasing activity was appropriate within his recovery plan.

Confidence matters

I learned that caregivers recover too. Fear of reinjury can persist after a dog begins showing steady improvement.

Capacity is not clearance

One successful weekend did not replace veterinary follow-up or prove complete healing. I kept those ideas separate.

WHAT I LEARNED

Progress looked different than I expected.

  • Small increases built confidence.
  • Watching Oliver mattered more than following fear.
  • Protecting him and rebuilding him eventually became different goals.
  • Real-world success should stay within veterinary limits.
