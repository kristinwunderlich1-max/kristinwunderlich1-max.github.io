WALKING AND CONFIDENCE

How did I know when it was time to let Oliver do more?

For weeks, my job was to prevent too much movement. Eventually, the harder question became whether I was still protecting Oliver—or holding him back after he had begun to show me he was ready for more.

This page describes how I interpreted Oliver's behavior within the recovery plan we had. It is not a walking schedule for another dog. Activity progression should come from your own veterinarian, surgeon, or rehabilitation professional.

THE SHORT ANSWER

I did not make the decision from one good walk.

I looked at the pattern: how Oliver moved, how he recovered afterward, whether he remained comfortable, whether his confidence was returning, and whether the activity still fit the guidance we had been given.

His physical ability and his medical clearance were related, but they were not identical. Oliver could sometimes do more than I felt ready to allow. At other times, I was so focused on preventing risk that I almost missed how much progress he had already made.

WHY WALKING MATTERED SO MUCH

Daily walks were part of Oliver's identity, not just exercise.

Before surgery, Oliver walked every day. Some walks were one mile. Some were five. He knew the routes, the sequence of the outing, and the role walking played in the rhythm of the day.

Restricting walking changed more than his activity level. It removed one of the routines that helped him understand what came next.

That is why the stroller became so useful. It let Oliver participate in the walk without asking his recovering leg to do the full work.

The real problem

Physical need
Protect healing tissue and rebuild strength gradually.
Routine need
Remain part of the daily walk.
Owner challenge
Know when caution was still useful and when it was becoming too restrictive.

WHAT I WATCHED

I paid attention to what happened during the walk and after it.

I looked at the quality of Oliver's movement rather than distance alone. Was he placing the leg? Was his gait becoming smoother? Did he slow down in a way that looked like ordinary fatigue, or did something change suddenly?

I also watched what happened later. A dog can look enthusiastic in the moment and still show that the activity was too much afterward.

I compared one day with the next instead of treating a single outing as proof that he was ready for a large increase.

THE SIGNALS THAT MATTERED TO ME

Progress was a pattern, not a milestone sticker.

How he used the leg

I watched whether Oliver placed weight through the leg, whether the movement looked more even, and whether he guarded it more after activity.

How he recovered afterward

I paid attention to rest, stiffness, hesitation, and whether the next bathroom trip or walk looked different.

Whether confidence was returning

Oliver's willingness to move through familiar routines mattered. Confidence did not replace medical guidance, but it told me something about how he was experiencing recovery.

Whether the change was repeatable

One strong day was encouraging. Several strong days without a concerning change gave me more confidence.

THE STROLLER PHASE

The stroller helped us preserve the routine while controlling the workload.

The stroller was already owned before TPLO. During recovery, it became one of the most useful tools I had.

Oliver could come with Baxter and me, experience the route, and remain included. I could let him walk the portion that fit the plan and ride for the rest.

That made the transition less abrupt. He did not have to go from no walking to a full normal walk, and I did not have to choose between total exclusion and too much activity.

THE LAKE CHANGED MY CONFIDENCE

Oliver showed me more ability than I had been allowing him to use.

During the July lake trip, Oliver moved through hours of normal life: visiting, walking between activities, playing in modified ways, and being part of the group.

He was not allowed to jump from the dock, but he still walked far more over the course of the trip than I had been allowing at home.

He handled it well.

The trip did not prove that every activity was safe. It did show me that my picture of Oliver's current ability had fallen behind the dog in front of me.

JULY 16, 2026

I let Oliver walk instead of putting him in the stroller.

By then, he was six weeks post-surgery.

My decision was not based only on the date. It came after seeing what he had managed at the lake and realizing that both his confidence and mine had changed.

I believed it was time to start rebuilding more deliberately instead of continuing to default to the most restrictive option.

That did not mean returning immediately to pre-surgery distances. It meant allowing a little more, watching carefully, and adjusting from there.

WHAT CHANGED MY THINKING

Protection and rebuilding are both parts of recovery.

Early in recovery, the safest decision was often to prevent movement. Later, continuing to prevent too much movement could create a different problem: less strength, less confidence, and a dog who was no longer practicing ordinary life.

I had to change roles.

I was no longer only the person stopping Oliver. I had to become the person helping him rebuild.

HOW I INCREASED ACTIVITY

I changed one thing at a time.

I did not increase distance, pace, terrain, duration, and freedom all at once.

A longer walk on the same easy route told me more than a longer walk combined with hills, excitement, slippery footing, and off-leash movement.

The questions I used

  • What exactly am I increasing?
  • Can I keep the route predictable?
  • What did he do comfortably yesterday?
  • How did he look later that day?
  • How did he look the next morning?
  • Am I changing the plan because of evidence—or because I am impatient or afraid?
  • Does this still fit the veterinary guidance we were given?

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY

I would define the transition from protection to rebuilding earlier.

I understood the early restrictions. I was less prepared for the middle stage, when Oliver was clearly improving but was not yet back to normal.

I would ask the veterinary team more specific questions in advance: what should change after the first recheck, what signs suggest the activity was too much, how to progress duration, and what kinds of movement matter most for rebuilding strength.

IF I WERE STARTING THIS PHASE AGAIN

I would keep the plan simple enough to observe clearly.

My practical approach

  • Follow the veterinary progression first
  • Use familiar, predictable routes
  • Increase only one variable at a time
  • Watch both gait and recovery afterward
  • Record meaningful changes
  • Preserve participation without forcing full distance
  • Call the veterinary team if something suddenly worsens

WHAT THIS PAGE CANNOT ANSWER

I cannot tell another owner how far or how long their dog should walk.

That depends on the surgery, healing stage, examination findings, gait, rehabilitation plan, pain control, age, conditioning, and individual dog.

Oliver's story is useful because it shows how my thinking changed—not because his six-week decision should become another dog's schedule.
